# Polson vs Hardin, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Polson and Hardin offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Polson is a scenic lakeside community with 5,335 people, its location on the southern shore of Flathead Lake, its cherry orchards, and quick access to Flathead Lake and the Mission Mountains. Hardin, with just 3,742 residents, is a agricultural community near the Crow Reservation known for its proximity to the Little Bighorn Battlefield, its farming and ranching economy, and the Bighorn River and Yellowtail Dam.

## Who Should Move to Polson?

* Retirees and remote workers seeking a beautiful lakeside setting with a slower pace of life.
* Boaters, anglers, and water sports enthusiasts who want immediate access to the largest natural freshwater lake in the West.
* People looking for a more affordable alternative to the resort towns of the northern Flathead Valley.
* Those who appreciate a community with a strong agricultural heritage, particularly its famous cherry harvest.

## Who Should Move to Hardin?

* Agricultural workers and farmers involved in the sugar beet and wheat industries of the Bighorn Valley.
* People seeking very affordable housing in a quiet, rural setting.
* History enthusiasts interested in the rich Native American culture and the legacy of the Little Bighorn.
* Anglers who want to be near the world-famous trout fishing below Yellowtail Dam.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Polson is around $415,000, while in Hardin it is closer to $204,573, a difference of roughly $210,427, making Hardin significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Polson averages about $1,050 per month, compared to roughly $726 in Hardin.

Polson's overall cost of living differs from Hardin's. Polson offers lower average salaries compared to Hardin, with a median household income of $52,500 compared to Hardin's $60,463.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Polson offers a relaxed, scenic lifestyle defined by its position on Flathead Lake. It lacks the polished resort feel of towns further north, maintaining a more grounded, agricultural character while still providing spectacular water and mountain access. Hardin is a quiet, culturally diverse agricultural center where the plains meet the Bighorn River. It is a town defined by farming, ranching, and its location bordering the Crow Indian Reservation, offering a slow-paced, rural lifestyle far from the state's major urban centers.
