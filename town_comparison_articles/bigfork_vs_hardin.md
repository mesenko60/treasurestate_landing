# Bigfork vs Hardin: Which Montana Town Is Right for You?

Bigfork and Hardin represent two of Montana's most distinct communities, separated not just by geography but by culture, landscape, and cost of living. Bigfork, A Village by the Bay on Flathead Lake in western Montana, is a resort-adjacent community of 5,000 people with a thriving arts scene, premium real estate, and access to Glacier National Park. Hardin, known as Where the Rivers Meet in southeastern Montana, is a town of 3,742 people near the confluence of the Bighorn and Little Bighorn rivers, known for the Little Bighorn Battlefield, the Crow Nation's cultural heritage, and the Bighorn River's world-class tailwater trout fishery.

## Who Should Move to Bigfork?

- Outdoor enthusiasts who want Flathead Lake, Glacier National Park, and a wide variety of fishing, boating, and hiking options
- Arts and culture lovers who want a vibrant summer theater and arts scene in a small-town setting
- Retirees or remote workers who want a beautiful lakeside community with a polished, welcoming atmosphere
- Those who want the full northwestern Montana experience, from skiing to lake recreation to wildlife watching

## Who Should Move to Hardin?

- History enthusiasts drawn to Little Bighorn Battlefield and the rich cultural heritage of the Crow Nation
- Anglers who want the Bighorn River, one of Montana's premier tailwater trout fisheries, close at hand
- People looking for very affordable housing and a low cost of living in a quiet, rural setting
- Those who prefer eastern Montana's wide-open prairie landscapes and a traditional, community-centered way of life

## Cost of Living Breakdown

The housing gap is enormous. The typical home value in Bigfork is $758,435 compared to just $204,573 in Hardin, a difference of over $550,000. Rent is also significantly higher in Bigfork at $1,103 per month versus $726 in Hardin. Bigfork's median household income of $82,648 is higher than Hardin's $60,463, but the housing cost gap is so large that Hardin is dramatically more affordable in real terms.

Bigfork is served by Glacier Park International Airport in Kalispell, 22 miles away. Hardin's nearest airport is in Billings, 54 miles to the west.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Bigfork has a polished, destination quality that draws visitors and retirees from across the country. The summer arts festival, the Bigfork Summer Playhouse, and the lakeside setting give it a vibrancy that is unusual for a town of its size. Hardin is quieter and more rooted in history and tradition. The Crow Fair each August, one of the largest Native American gatherings in the country, gives Hardin a cultural richness that is unique in Montana. These two towns share a state but almost nothing else in terms of landscape, culture, or daily rhythm.

Bigfork has 176 recreational sites within 30 miles, including 28 fishing access points, 32 lakes, 25 trailheads, and access to Glacier National Park. Hardin has 20 sites, anchored by the Bighorn River, 10 fishing access points, and the Little Bighorn Battlefield historic site. For sheer recreational volume and variety, Bigfork is in a different league. For history, river fishing, and cultural depth, Hardin offers something genuinely distinctive.
