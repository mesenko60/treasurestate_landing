# Bigfork vs Big Sky: Which Montana Town Is Right for You?

Bigfork and Big Sky are both premier outdoor destinations in Montana, but they attract very different residents and offer very different lifestyles. Bigfork, A Village by the Bay on Flathead Lake, is a lakeside arts community of 5,000 people with a vibrant summer culture, world-class fishing, and access to Glacier National Park. Big Sky, The Biggest Skiing in America in the Gallatin Canyon, is a resort community of 3,591 people built around one of the largest ski mountains in North America, with some of the highest home prices in the state.

## Who Should Move to Bigfork?

- Outdoor enthusiasts who want Flathead Lake, Glacier National Park, and a wide variety of fishing, boating, and hiking options all within reach
- Arts and culture lovers who want a vibrant summer theater and arts scene in a small-town lakeside setting
- Retirees or remote workers who want a beautiful community with a polished, welcoming year-round atmosphere
- Those who want the full northwestern Montana experience at a fraction of the cost of a resort community

## Who Should Move to Big Sky?

- Skiers and snowboarders who want one of the largest ski resorts in North America as a literal backyard
- High-income remote workers, retirees, or second-home buyers who want a luxury mountain lifestyle in the Gallatin Canyon
- Outdoor enthusiasts who want access to Yellowstone National Park, the Gallatin River, and world-class hiking and hot springs
- People who want a rapidly growing resort community with excellent restaurants and a sophisticated year-round energy

## Cost of Living Breakdown

The housing gap between these two towns is enormous. The typical home value in Big Sky is $1,819,905 compared to $758,435 in Bigfork, a difference of over $1 million. Rent is also significantly higher in Big Sky at $1,712 per month versus $1,103 in Bigfork. Big Sky's median household income of $103,625 is higher than Bigfork's $82,648, reflecting the resort economy and the high-earning remote workers it attracts. Even accounting for higher incomes, Big Sky's cost of living is in a category of its own.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Bigfork has a relaxed, lakeside energy that draws artists, retirees, and outdoor enthusiasts who want beauty and community without the resort price tag. The Bigfork Summer Playhouse, the annual arts festival, and Flathead Lake give it a cultural richness that is unusual for a town of its size. Big Sky is more intense and more expensive, a place that has grown dramatically in recent years as wealthy transplants and remote workers have discovered the Gallatin Canyon. Both towns are outstanding places to live for those who can afford them, but Bigfork offers a more accessible version of the Montana dream.

Bigfork has 176 recreational sites within 30 miles, including 28 fishing access points, 32 lakes, 25 trailheads, and access to Glacier National Park. Big Sky has 140 sites, anchored by Big Sky Resort, 3 hot springs, 23 trailheads, and 17 waterfalls. Both towns are exceptional for outdoor recreation, but they offer very different landscapes and experiences.
