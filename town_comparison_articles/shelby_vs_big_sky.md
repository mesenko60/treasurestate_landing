# Shelby vs Big Sky, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Shelby and Big Sky offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Shelby is a historic railroad and agricultural town with 3,169 people, its position on the Hi-Line, its famous 1923 Dempsey-Gibbons heavyweight title fight, and quick access to the Sweet Grass Hills and Lake Elwell. Big Sky, with just 3,591 residents, is a premier alpine resort community known for the massive Big Sky Resort, its luxury real estate market, and Lone Mountain and the Gallatin Canyon.

## Who Should Move to Shelby?

* Workers in the agriculture, railroad, or energy sectors looking for steady employment on the Hi-Line.
* People seeking very affordable housing in a quiet, rural setting.
* Those who appreciate the stark beauty of the northern plains and the close-knit community of a small agricultural town.
* History buffs interested in the unique legacy of the 1923 heavyweight championship fight.

## Who Should Move to Big Sky?

* Affluent buyers and second-home owners looking for luxury alpine real estate.
* Dedicated skiers and snowboarders who want access to some of the biggest and best terrain in North America.
* Hospitality and resort workers who are willing to navigate the high cost of living for the mountain lifestyle.
* Outdoor enthusiasts who want to live in a high-altitude environment with immediate access to wilderness and Yellowstone National Park.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Shelby is around $185,000, while in Big Sky it is closer to $1,819,905, a difference of roughly $1,634,905, making Shelby significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Shelby averages about $700 per month, compared to roughly $1,712 in Big Sky.

Shelby's overall cost of living differs from Big Sky's. Shelby offers lower average salaries compared to Big Sky, with a median household income of $58,000 compared to Big Sky's $103,625.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Shelby is a resilient, hardworking town on the northern plains, where life is shaped by the railroad, agriculture, and the vast open spaces of the Hi-Line. It offers a quiet, practical lifestyle far removed from the state's major urban centers. Big Sky is not a traditional town but a sprawling, high-end resort community built around outdoor recreation and luxury living. The atmosphere is transient and seasonal, driven by tourism, skiing, and wealth, offering spectacular alpine access but lacking the historic, grounded feel of older Montana settlements.
