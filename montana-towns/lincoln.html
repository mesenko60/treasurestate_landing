Lincoln, Montana

Gateway to Wilderness Adventure

Quick Facts

  • Population: 1,013
  • County: Lewis and Clark County
  • Founded: 1865
  • Elevation: 4,536 ft
  • Known For: Outdoor recreation, Blackfoot Pathways: Sculpture in the Wild, gateway to wilderness areas
  • Nearby Landmarks: Blackfoot River, Continental Divide, Rogers Pass, Scapegoat Wilderness Area
  • Fun Fact: Rogers Pass, just 18 miles northeast of Lincoln, recorded the coldest temperature in the continental United States at -70°F (-57°C) on January 20, 1954

Notable People & Pop Culture

  • Doug Swingley – Four-time Iditarod Trail Sled Dog Race champion (1995, 1999, 2000, 2001) and the first non-Alaskan to win the prestigious 1,100-mile race
  • Ted Kaczynski – Infamous "Unabomber" who lived in a remote cabin near Lincoln until his arrest in 1996, bringing national attention to the small town
  • "Manhunt: Unabomber" – Discovery Channel series that featured Lincoln and the surrounding area in its portrayal of the FBI investigation that led to Kaczynski's capture

  • ✧ Blackfoot River Adventures – Cast a line for trophy trout or navigate thrilling rapids on one of Montana's most celebrated waterways, made famous in "A River Runs Through It"
  • ✧ Scapegoat Wilderness Exploration – Trek into 239,936 acres of pristine wilderness featuring alpine lakes, rugged peaks, and abundant wildlife viewing opportunities
  • ✧ Continental Divide Trail – Access this iconic 3,100-mile national scenic trail that passes near Lincoln, offering day hikes or multi-day backpacking adventures

Local Industry & Economy

Lincoln's economy has evolved from its gold rush origins to a diverse blend of traditional and modern enterprises. While the historical foundations of logging and mining continue to provide employment, the town has increasingly embraced its role as a gateway to outdoor recreation. Tourism now forms a vital economic pillar, with outfitters, guides, and hospitality businesses catering to visitors drawn by world-class fishing, hunting, hiking, and winter sports. The Blackfoot Pathways: Sculpture in the Wild has emerged as a cultural and economic asset, attracting art enthusiasts year-round. Small businesses, including specialty shops, restaurants, and accommodation providers, form the backbone of the local economy, while the Lincoln Valley Chamber of Commerce actively promotes community growth through events and tourism initiatives. This economic resilience, balancing natural resource traditions with recreation and tourism, reflects Lincoln's adaptability in Montana's changing rural landscape.

History & Heritage

Lincoln's story begins along ancient Native American pathways, where the "River of the Road to the Buffalo" guided travelers for centuries before Meriwether Lewis passed through in 1806. The town's modern chapter opened in 1865 when prospectors David Culp and Thomas Patterson staked a claim in what they named Abe Lincoln Gulch, honoring the recently assassinated president. As word of gold discoveries spread, fortune-seekers flocked to the area, establishing a settlement that would eventually be known simply as Lincoln.

The town's permanence was cemented with the establishment of a post office in 1887, and historic structures like the Lincoln Community Hall (1918) and Hotel Lincoln (1914)—both now listed on the National Register of Historical Places—stand as testaments to the community's enduring spirit. Throughout the 20th century, Lincoln evolved as a commercial hub supporting the surrounding wilderness areas, with logging and mining operations complementing recreational activities along the Blackfoot River.

In more recent history, Lincoln gained unexpected national attention in 1996 when federal agents arrested Ted Kaczynski, the notorious "Unabomber," at his remote cabin south of town. In 2017, the community demonstrated its resilience once again when it weathered a 5.8-magnitude earthquake—the eighth-strongest recorded in Montana history. Through gold rushes, economic shifts, and natural challenges, Lincoln has maintained its character as a quintessential Montana mountain town, where the past remains palpable amid the breathtaking natural surroundings.

Seasonal Activities & Local Events

  • Spring/Summer: Fly fishing on the legendary Blackfoot River, hiking the Continental Divide Trail, mountain biking through forest trails, wildlife photography, and exploring Blackfoot Pathways: Sculpture in the Wild
  • Fall/Winter: World-class snowmobiling on hundreds of miles of groomed trails, cross-country skiing, ice fishing, winter wildlife viewing, and hunting in some of Montana's most pristine wilderness areas
  • Annual Events: Fourth of July celebration featuring a small-town parade and spectacular fireworks display, Lincoln Open Rod & Gun Club Shooting Competitions, and community gatherings at the historic Lincoln Community Hall

Getting There & Nearby Destinations

Lincoln sits along Montana Highway 200, the longest state highway in the United States at 706.6 miles. The town is positioned 87 miles northeast of Great Falls and 77 miles west of Missoula, making it accessible yet perfectly secluded. Travelers from Helena can reach Lincoln via Highway 279 through the stunning Stemple Pass, while those seeking adventure can approach from Rogers Pass on Highway 200, famous for recording the coldest temperature in the continental United States. Nearby destinations include the charming mining town of Helena (60 miles southeast), the outdoor recreation hub of Missoula, and the gateway communities to Glacier National Park further north.
