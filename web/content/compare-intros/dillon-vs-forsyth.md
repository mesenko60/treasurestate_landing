# Forsyth vs Dillon, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Forsyth and Dillon offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Forsyth is a historic railroad town on the Yellowstone River with 1,630 people, its beautiful, historic county courthouse, its position as a transportation and agricultural hub, and quick access to the Yellowstone River and the eastern plains. Dillon, with 3,880 residents, is a historic ranching and college town known for the University of Montana Western, its deep agricultural roots, and the Beaverhead River and surrounding Pioneer Mountains.

## Who Should Move to Forsyth?

* Workers in agriculture, transportation, or local services looking for a stable community.
* People seeking very affordable housing and a low overall cost of living.
* Those who appreciate a traditional, slow-paced rural lifestyle on the eastern plains.
* Hunters and anglers who want uncrowded access to the Yellowstone River.

## Who Should Move to Dillon?

* Students, faculty, and staff associated with the University of Montana Western.
* Ranchers and agricultural workers who want to live in a community that still deeply values its livestock heritage.
* Anglers seeking world-class trout fishing on the Beaverhead and Big Hole rivers.
* People looking for a genuine, unpretentious western town with a stable economy and access to vast public lands.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Forsyth is around $208,690, while in Dillon it is closer to $375,585, a difference of roughly $166,895, making Forsyth significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Forsyth averages about $743 per month, compared to roughly $821 in Dillon.

Forsyth's overall cost of living differs from Dillon's. Forsyth offers higher average salaries compared to Dillon, with a median household income of $58,375 compared to Dillon's $51,458.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Forsyth is a quiet, practical community in eastern Montana, defined by the railroad, agriculture, and the Yellowstone River. It offers a slow-paced, highly affordable lifestyle with a strong sense of local history. Dillon strikes a unique balance between a traditional cowboy town and a small college community. It remains deeply tied to the ranching industry, but the presence of the university adds a layer of cultural and educational activity, creating a grounded, authentic Montana atmosphere.
