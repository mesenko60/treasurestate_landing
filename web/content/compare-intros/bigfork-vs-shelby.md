# Shelby vs Bigfork, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Shelby and Bigfork offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Shelby is a historic railroad and agricultural town with 3,169 people, its position on the Hi-Line, its famous 1923 Dempsey-Gibbons heavyweight title fight, and quick access to the Sweet Grass Hills and Lake Elwell. Bigfork, with just 5,000 residents, is a picturesque village on Flathead Lake known for its thriving arts scene, its cherry orchards, and Flathead Lake and the Swan River.

## Who Should Move to Shelby?

* Workers in the agriculture, railroad, or energy sectors looking for steady employment on the Hi-Line.
* People seeking very affordable housing in a quiet, rural setting.
* Those who appreciate the stark beauty of the northern plains and the close-knit community of a small agricultural town.
* History buffs interested in the unique legacy of the 1923 heavyweight championship fight.

## Who Should Move to Bigfork?

* Retirees and affluent professionals looking for a scenic, upscale lakeside community.
* Boaters, sailors, and water sports enthusiasts who want to live on the largest natural freshwater lake in the West.
* Art lovers who appreciate the town's galleries, summer playhouse, and cultural events.
* People who desire a resort-like atmosphere with high-end dining and boutique shopping.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Shelby is around $185,000, while in Bigfork it is closer to $758,435, a difference of roughly $573,435, making Shelby significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Shelby averages about $700 per month, compared to roughly $1,103 in Bigfork.

Shelby's overall cost of living differs from Bigfork's. Shelby offers lower average salaries compared to Bigfork, with a median household income of $58,000 compared to Bigfork's $82,648.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Shelby is a resilient, hardworking town on the northern plains, where life is shaped by the railroad, agriculture, and the vast open spaces of the Hi-Line. It offers a quiet, practical lifestyle far removed from the state's major urban centers. Bigfork feels like a curated resort village, where life centers around the water, the arts, and seasonal tourism. The downtown is polished and walkable, filled with galleries and cafes, offering a lifestyle that is significantly more upscale and leisure-focused than most Montana towns.
