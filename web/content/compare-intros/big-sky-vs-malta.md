# Malta vs Big Sky, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Malta and Big Sky offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Malta is a historic agricultural town on the Hi-Line with 2,100 people, its world-class dinosaur fossils, its deep ranching heritage, and quick access to the Bowdoin National Wildlife Refuge and the Missouri River Breaks. Big Sky, with 3,591 residents, is a premier alpine resort community known for the massive Big Sky Resort, its luxury real estate market, and Lone Mountain and the Gallatin Canyon.

## Who Should Move to Malta?

* Workers in agriculture, government, or local services looking for a quiet, stable community.
* People seeking very affordable housing and a low overall cost of living.
* Hunters, birders, and outdoor enthusiasts drawn to the vast, uncrowded public lands of northeastern Montana.
* Those who appreciate a traditional, slow-paced rural lifestyle on the high plains.

## Who Should Move to Big Sky?

* Affluent buyers and second-home owners looking for luxury alpine real estate.
* Dedicated skiers and snowboarders who want access to some of the biggest and best terrain in North America.
* Hospitality and resort workers who are willing to navigate the high cost of living for the mountain lifestyle.
* Outdoor enthusiasts who want to live in a high-altitude environment with immediate access to wilderness and Yellowstone National Park.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Malta is around $244,540, while in Big Sky it is closer to $1,819,905, a difference of roughly $1,575,365, making Malta significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Malta averages about $693 per month, compared to roughly $1,712 in Big Sky.

Malta's overall cost of living differs from Big Sky's. Malta offers lower average salaries compared to Big Sky, with a median household income of $59,449 compared to Big Sky's $103,625.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Malta is a quiet, resilient community on the eastern Hi-Line, defined by agriculture, wide-open spaces, and its unique paleontology attractions. It offers a slow-paced, highly affordable lifestyle far from the state's urban centers. Big Sky is not a traditional town but a sprawling, high-end resort community built around outdoor recreation and luxury living. The atmosphere is transient and seasonal, driven by tourism, skiing, and wealth, offering spectacular alpine access but lacking the historic, grounded feel of older Montana settlements.
