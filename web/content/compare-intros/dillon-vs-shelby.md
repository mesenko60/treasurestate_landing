# Shelby vs Dillon, Montana: Which Town Is Right for You?

Shelby and Dillon offer two very different Montana lifestyles. Shelby is a historic railroad and agricultural town with 3,169 people, its position on the Hi-Line, its famous 1923 Dempsey-Gibbons heavyweight title fight, and quick access to the Sweet Grass Hills and Lake Elwell. Dillon, with just 3,880 residents, is a historic ranching and college town known for the University of Montana Western, its deep agricultural roots, and the Beaverhead River and surrounding Pioneer Mountains.

## Who Should Move to Shelby?

* Workers in the agriculture, railroad, or energy sectors looking for steady employment on the Hi-Line.
* People seeking very affordable housing in a quiet, rural setting.
* Those who appreciate the stark beauty of the northern plains and the close-knit community of a small agricultural town.
* History buffs interested in the unique legacy of the 1923 heavyweight championship fight.

## Who Should Move to Dillon?

* Students, faculty, and staff associated with the University of Montana Western.
* Ranchers and agricultural workers who want to live in a community that still deeply values its livestock heritage.
* Anglers seeking world-class trout fishing on the Beaverhead and Big Hole rivers.
* People looking for a genuine, unpretentious western town with a stable economy and access to vast public lands.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Housing is where the biggest difference shows up. The median home price in Shelby is around $185,000, while in Dillon it is closer to $375,585, a difference of roughly $190,585, making Shelby significantly more affordable. Rent follows the same pattern: a typical rental in Shelby averages about $700 per month, compared to roughly $821 in Dillon.

Shelby's overall cost of living differs from Dillon's. Shelby offers higher average salaries compared to Dillon, with a median household income of $58,000 compared to Dillon's $51,458.

## Lifestyle and Vibe

Shelby is a resilient, hardworking town on the northern plains, where life is shaped by the railroad, agriculture, and the vast open spaces of the Hi-Line. It offers a quiet, practical lifestyle far removed from the state's major urban centers. Dillon strikes a unique balance between a traditional cowboy town and a small college community. It remains deeply tied to the ranching industry, but the presence of the university adds a layer of cultural and educational activity, creating a grounded, authentic Montana atmosphere.
