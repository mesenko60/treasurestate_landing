# Ubet
## History

The name Ubet was the kind of name that the frontier generated with a certain casual wit, a contraction of "you bet" that reflected the confidence of the man who established the settlement in 1880. A.R. Barrows chose the name for his stage stop on the Fort Benton to Billings route, and the choice said something about the character of the place and the era: a bet on the future of central Montana, placed by a man who believed the country was going somewhere and wanted to be positioned along the route when it got there.

Barrows built his stage stop at a location that made practical sense for the overland traffic of the early 1880s. The Fort Benton to Billings route was one of the primary arteries of the Montana Territory, connecting the Missouri River steamboat terminus at Fort Benton with the Yellowstone country to the south. Stage lines carried passengers, mail, and express freight along this route, and the stations that supported them were essential infrastructure in a territory where the distances between settlements were measured in days of travel rather than hours. Ubet's position along this route gave it a guaranteed clientele as long as the stage traffic continued.

The settlement that grew up around Barrows's stage stop was modest but functional. Ubet included a post office, a two-story hotel, a blacksmith shop, an ice house, and a saloon, the standard complement of services for a stage station that aspired to be something more than a relay point. The hotel's two stories were a sign of ambition, suggesting that Barrows expected overnight guests as well as the transient traffic of the stage coaches. The ice house was a practical necessity in a country where summer temperatures could be brutal and the preservation of food and drink required more than a cool cellar.

The community that used these services was drawn from the ranching operations that were beginning to establish themselves in the Judith Basin and the surrounding country. The open range cattle era of the 1880s brought large herds into central Montana, and the cowboys and ranchers who worked them needed the services that Ubet provided. The stage stop became a social center for a dispersed rural population that had few other gathering places, and the saloon and hotel served functions that went beyond their commercial purposes.

The arrival of the railroad in central Montana changed the economics of stage travel fundamentally. When the Montana Central Railroad pushed through the Judith Basin in the early 1890s, the stage routes that had been the primary means of long-distance travel became obsolete almost overnight. The railroad could move passengers and freight faster, cheaper, and more reliably than any stage line, and the communities that had grown up to serve the stage traffic found themselves stranded off the new transportation network. Ubet was one of those communities.

The post office closed, the hotel emptied, and the commercial functions that had made Ubet a viable settlement migrated to the railroad towns that were growing along the new line. The blacksmith shop and the saloon held on for a few years, sustained by the local ranching trade, but the community's identity as a stage stop was gone, and without that identity it had no particular reason to exist. The homestead era brought a new wave of settlers into the Judith Basin in the early twentieth century, but the homesteaders built their communities around the railroad towns rather than the old stage stops, and Ubet was left behind.

The physical evidence of Ubet's existence has largely disappeared. The two-story hotel, the blacksmith shop, and the other structures that Barrows built were constructed of wood, and wood does not last long in the Montana climate without maintenance. The site has been returned to agricultural use, and the only markers of the settlement's existence are the historical records that document it and the occasional artifact that turns up in the soil.

Ubet's story is representative of a particular type of Montana ghost town: the stage stop that served a vital function during the territorial era and became obsolete when the railroad arrived. Dozens of such communities existed along the major stage routes of the 1870s and 1880s, and most of them vanished as completely as Ubet when the stage traffic ended. They are ghost towns in the most complete sense of the term, places where even the physical evidence of habitation has been erased by time and the practical demands of agricultural land use.

The Judith Basin that surrounded Ubet was one of the most productive cattle ranges in Montana during the open range era. The basin's grass was deep and nutritious, the winters were less severe than the high mountain country to the west, and the Judith River and its tributaries provided reliable water for the large herds that grazed the range. The great cattle outfits of the 1880s, including the DHS Ranch and the Judith Basin Pool, ran tens of thousands of cattle across the country that Ubet served, and the stage stop was a regular stopping point for the cowboys and ranchers who worked those operations.

The winter of 1886-1887 was the event that ended the open range era in the Judith Basin, as it did across much of Montana. The cattle that had grazed the range in their hundreds of thousands died by the tens of thousands in the blizzards and cold that swept the northern plains that winter, and the ranchers who survived the catastrophe rebuilt their operations on a more conservative model, with smaller herds, hay reserves, and fenced pastures. The open range was gone, and the stage stop at Ubet, which had served the open range economy, found itself serving a different and smaller cattle industry.

## What Remains Today

Very little survives at the Ubet townsite. The location is in agricultural use, and the structures that once housed the stage stop's commercial functions are gone. The site is on private land, and there are no interpretive markers or public access facilities.

The broader Judith Basin area retains more substantial ghost town remnants at Kendall, Maiden, and Gilt Edge in the Judith Mountains, and at the various homestead-era communities that dot the basin. These sites provide a more rewarding ghost town experience for visitors to the area, and Ubet is primarily of interest to historians researching the stage route era of central Montana.

## Visiting

Ubet was located in Fergus County in the Judith Basin, along the historic Fort Benton to Billings stage route. The general area is accessible from US Highway 191, which follows roughly the same north-south corridor through central Montana that the old stage route used. Lewistown, the Fergus County seat, is the nearest city with visitor services and is located approximately in the center of the basin.

The Central Montana Museum in Lewistown maintains exhibits on the history of the Judith Basin, including the stage route era and the mining and homestead periods that followed. The museum is the best starting point for understanding the history of the area and for obtaining information about the locations of the various ghost towns and historical sites in Fergus County.
