# Rimini
## History

The mining district that became Rimini was one of the oldest in Montana, its first lode claim staked in 1864 by a prospector named John Caplice, who had come to the territory in the wake of the Alder Gulch gold rush and turned his attention to the hard-rock silver and lead deposits in the mountains northwest of Helena. The early workers called the camp Young Ireland, a name that reflected the composition of the workforce: Irish immigrants who had come to Montana as they had come to every American mining district, following the work wherever it led.

The ore was there, but the logistics of getting it out were difficult. The mountains northwest of Helena were steep and forested, the roads rudimentary, and the cost of hauling ore to the smelters in Helena or Butte ate deeply into whatever the mine could produce. The district remained a modest operation through the 1870s, its roughly dozen lode claims producing gold, silver, lead, and zinc in quantities that were interesting but not enough to attract serious capital. The Lee Mountain Mine, Caplice's original claim, was the most productive of the early operations, but even it could not generate the kind of returns that would attract the capital needed to develop the district at scale.

What changed the equation was the railroad. The Northern Pacific Railroad extended a branch line to Rimini in the mid-1880s, a ten-mile spur that connected the mining district to the main line and reduced the cost of ore transport by more than half compared to wagon hauls. The effect was immediate. Workers arrived in larger numbers, new claims were staked, and the population of the camp grew to several hundred by 1890. The Hotel Rimini opened to serve the miners and the occasional traveler. A post office was established in 1884. A main street of commercial establishments took shape along the drainage. The district's roughly one hundred and fifty mines began operating in earnest, extracting lead, zinc, and silver ores that were shipped to the smelters at East Helena for processing.

The cumulative mineral output of the Rimini district reached approximately seven million dollars during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, a figure that was respectable but not spectacular by the standards of Montana's silver boom. The district never produced the kind of concentrated wealth that Granite or Marysville did, and it never attracted the same scale of capital investment. What it had was persistence: the mines operated intermittently through the 1920s and into the 1930s, sustained by modest but consistent yields and by the willingness of a small community to stay in a place that the larger world had largely forgotten.

The population peaked at around three hundred in 1890, fell to about one hundred and fifty-five by 1900, and continued declining through the early twentieth century. By 1920, only eighty-five people remained in the district. The mines closed one by one as the high-grade ore bodies were exhausted and the economics of working the lower-grade material ceased to make sense. The camp that had been called Young Ireland and then Rimini, named after the Italian city on the Adriatic coast for reasons that no one now remembers clearly, became a semi-ghost town, a place with a handful of permanent residents and a history considerably larger than its present.

Rimini has an additional chapter that most Montana ghost towns lack. During World War II, the U.S. Army established Camp Rimini on land adjacent to the mining district and used it as a training facility for sled dog teams destined for Arctic and Alaskan operations. The Army's Quartermaster Corps trained hundreds of dogs and their handlers at the camp between 1942 and 1944, and the site became one of the principal centers for military working dog training in the country during those years. The dogs trained at Camp Rimini were deployed to Alaska, Greenland, and other Arctic theaters, where they served as transportation and search-and-rescue animals in terrain that no motorized vehicle could navigate. The program was part of a broader Army effort to develop capabilities for winter warfare, and the mountains northwest of Helena provided the terrain and climate conditions required for realistic training. The camp was decommissioned after the war, and the buildings were eventually removed, but the history of it is preserved in the records of the Army's wartime logistics operations and in the memories of the families of the men who trained there.

There is a darker legacy as well. The Upper Tenmile Creek Mining Area, which encompasses the Rimini district, was designated a Superfund site by the Environmental Protection Agency due to acid mine drainage contaminating the watershed with heavy metals. The legacy of a century of mining is written in the chemistry of the streams that drain the district, and the EPA has been conducting remediation work in the area for decades, attempting to undo what the mines left behind.

The Superfund designation brought federal attention and resources to a problem that the district's small remaining population could not have addressed on its own. Remediation work has included the removal of mine waste from stream banks, the construction of water treatment systems, and the revegetation of disturbed areas. The work is ongoing, and the chemistry of the watershed continues to improve, though the process of undoing a century of mining is measured in decades rather than years.

## What Remains Today

Scattered cabins, mine ruins, and interpretive sites remain in the forested terrain of the Rimini district. The area is accessible to the public and is used for hiking and exploration. The Superfund designation means that some areas are subject to ongoing remediation work, and visitors should be aware of the environmental conditions.

A small number of permanent residents, approximately fifty by the 2020 census, continue to live in the area, making Rimini one of the more inhabited of Montana's ghost towns.

## Visiting

Rimini is located approximately twelve miles northwest of Helena within the Helena-Lewis and Clark National Forest. The access road is maintained and passable for standard vehicles in summer. The area is used for hiking, mountain biking, and exploration of the mining ruins. No formal visitor services exist at the site.

The EPA's remediation work in the Upper Tenmile Creek watershed has created some interpretive signage explaining the environmental history of the district.
