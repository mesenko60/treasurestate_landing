# Sterling
Sterling sits in Madison County in the valley of Norwegian Gulch, a drainage in the Tobacco Root Mountains where placer gold was discovered in the spring of 1864 and where the Clark Mill was built in 1866 to process the lode ore that followed the placers. The town that grew up around the mill took its name from the quality of the ore it processed, a reference to the sterling silver that the district's mines produced alongside their gold. The Upper Hot Springs Mining District, of which Sterling was the commercial center, was never among the great producers of southwestern Montana, but it was a real working district that sustained a community for several decades and left behind the stone ruins of the Clark Mill as a visible record of its ambitions.

## History

The Upper Hot Springs Mining District opened in the spring of 1864, when placer gold was discovered in Norwegian Gulch and along South Meadow Creek in the Tobacco Root Mountains of Madison County. The discovery was part of the broader wave of prospecting that followed the Alder Gulch rush of 1863, as miners who had arrived too late for the best ground in the main gulch fanned out through the surrounding ranges looking for the next discovery. Norwegian Gulch yielded approximately $150,000 in gold by 1874, a respectable but not spectacular return that reflected the character of the district: productive enough to sustain a working community, but not rich enough to trigger the kind of rush that had overwhelmed Alder Gulch.

The town of Sterling developed as the district's commercial center, taking shape in the years after the initial placer discoveries as lode mining began to supplement the placer operations. The Clark Mill, built in 1866 to process the gold ore from the district's lode mines, was the most substantial structure in the community and the investment that gave Sterling its reason for existence. The mill's construction represented a transition from the individual-scale placer mining of the early rush to the more capital-intensive lode mining that characterized the second phase of most Montana mining districts. Where a placer miner needed a pan, a rocker, and a sluice box, a lode miner needed a shaft, a hoist, a mill, and the capital to build and operate all of it.

A post office was established at Sterling, giving the town an official existence that its neighbor Brandon, a few miles to the south in the same general district, never achieved. The post office was a marker of permanence and a practical necessity for a community that needed to maintain connections with the outside world, and its establishment at Sterling rather than at any of the other small camps in the district reflected the town's role as the district's administrative and commercial center.

The mining history of the Upper Hot Springs district followed the pattern common to most of Madison County's smaller camps. The early placer operations were worked out within a decade of the initial discovery, and the district's subsequent history depended on the lode mines and the mill that processed their ore. The Clark Mill processed ore from the district's gold and silver lode properties, and the town of Sterling provided the housing, commerce, and services that the mine workers required. The district was never large enough to attract the major corporate investors who transformed camps like Granite and Elkhorn into industrial operations, and it remained a small-scale working district throughout its productive life.

The collapse of silver prices in 1893 affected Sterling as it affected every silver-producing district in Montana, and the camp's population contracted sharply in the years that followed. Some operations continued into the twentieth century, working the gold-bearing portions of the district's ore bodies when silver prices made silver mining uneconomical, but the scale of operations never recovered to the levels of the district's peak years. The Clark Mill eventually fell silent, and the community that had grown up around it dispersed.

The Norwegian Gulch placer deposits that had opened the district in 1864 were reworked periodically in the twentieth century, as rising gold prices and new processing technologies made it economical to recover gold from ground that earlier miners had considered worked out. The dredging and hydraulic operations that reworked many of Montana's placer districts left their marks on Norwegian Gulch as they did elsewhere, and the creek today shows the characteristic disturbance of ground that has been processed multiple times over more than a century.

The Hot Springs area of Madison County, where Sterling was located, had a character somewhat different from the high-altitude mining camps of the Pioneer or Tobacco Root ranges. The hot springs themselves, which gave the district its name, were a natural feature of the landscape that attracted attention from the earliest settlers, and the combination of mineral springs and mining activity gave the area a dual identity that was unusual in the Montana mining landscape. The springs were used by miners for bathing and by the occasional traveler passing through, and they contributed to the area's reputation as a place with resources beyond the gold and silver in the ground.

The Madison Valley that surrounds the Sterling site is one of the most productive agricultural valleys in southwestern Montana, and the ranching economy that developed in the valley in the late nineteenth century provided a degree of stability that purely mining communities lacked. When the mines at Sterling played out and the Clark Mill fell silent, the valley's ranchers remained, and the landscape that had been shaped by mining gradually returned to agricultural use. The stone ruins of the Clark Mill stand in a valley that is now primarily ranch land, a reminder of the mining era that preceded the agricultural economy that has sustained the Madison Valley for more than a century.

## What Remains Today

The ruins of the Clark Mill are the most visible surviving feature of the Sterling site, the stone walls of the 1866 structure standing in the valley as a record of the district's lode mining era. The mill ruins have been photographed and documented by historians of Montana's mining heritage and are a recognizable landmark in the Norwegian Gulch area. The town site itself has been largely reclaimed by the landscape, with the foundations and debris of the mining era visible to those who know where to look.

## Visiting

Sterling is located in Madison County in the Norwegian Gulch area of the Tobacco Root Mountains, accessible via secondary roads from Sheridan or Twin Bridges. The roads are unpaved and conditions vary by season. The Clark Mill ruins are visible from the road. Twin Bridges, the Madison County seat, is the nearest service center with the Madison County Museum. The Tobacco Root Mountains offer hiking and backcountry access in summer, and the broader Madison Valley corridor is one of the most historically rich areas in southwestern Montana.
