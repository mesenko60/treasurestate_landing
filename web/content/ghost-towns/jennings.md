# Jennings
## History

The Kootenai River in the years before the Great Northern Railway reached the Montana-Idaho border was a transportation corridor of surprising importance. The river was navigable by steamboat for a significant portion of its length, and the boats that worked the Kootenai connected the communities along its banks to the larger economy of the Pacific Northwest. Jennings was one of the communities that grew up at the intersection of the steamboat trade and the overland routes that crossed the Kootenai country, and for a period around the turn of the twentieth century it was a place of genuine commercial activity.

The community took its name from the Jennings family, early settlers who established themselves in the Kootenai valley in the period before the railroad arrived. The location that would become Jennings was a natural stopping point for travelers moving through the valley, and the combination of the river crossing and the overland routes that converged at the site gave it a commercial logic that attracted the small businesses and services that a frontier community required.

The steamboat era on the Kootenai River was brief but significant. The boats that plied the river between Jennings, Montana, and Fort Steele, British Columbia, carried supplies to the mining and logging operations in the upper Kootenai country and returned with ore, timber, and the other products of the northern frontier. Bill Doak, who served as purser on one of these steamboats, later recalled the river trade as a period of genuine commercial vitality, when the Kootenai was a working waterway connecting communities that had no other reliable connection to the outside world. The steamboat captains who navigated the river's rapids and shallows were skilled men, and the boats they operated were the lifeline of the communities they served.

The Great Northern Railway's arrival in the Kootenai country in the 1890s transformed the transportation geography of the region in ways that both helped and hurt Jennings. The railroad provided a faster and more reliable connection to markets than the steamboats could offer, and it made the communities along its route more accessible to the broader economy. But the railroad also made the steamboat trade obsolete, and the commercial activity that had been concentrated at the river crossing points shifted to the railroad stations. Jennings was fortunate to have a station on the Great Northern line, which gave it continued relevance in the new transportation geography.

The community's peak activity came in the years around the turn of the century, when the combination of the railroad station and the residual steamboat trade made Jennings a point of commercial concentration in the Kootenai valley. The Montana Memory Project records indicate that Jennings thrived with steamboat business at this period, and the community had the commercial and social infrastructure of a functioning small town. The post office, the school, and the commercial establishments that served the surrounding population were the markers of a community that expected to persist.

The decline of Jennings followed the pattern of the other small communities in the Kootenai valley. The consolidation of the timber industry, the decline of agricultural settlement, and the broader rural depopulation of the mid-twentieth century all reduced the population of the valley communities. The construction of Libby Dam in the 1970s, which created the Lake Koocanusa reservoir, transformed the physical landscape of the Kootenai valley and ended the existence of several communities that occupied the land that would be inundated.

The history of Jennings reflects the particular character of the transportation frontier in the northern Rockies. The community existed at the intersection of two transportation systems, the steamboat trade and the railroad, and its fortunes were tied to the health of both. When the steamboat era ended and the railroad consolidated its dominance of the valley's commerce, Jennings retained enough of its railroad function to survive for a period. But the forces that were reducing the population of the northern Rockies in the twentieth century were stronger than the community's commercial logic, and Jennings eventually joined the list of Kootenai valley communities that exist only in the historical record.

The Kootenai River itself was a more formidable waterway than its modest reputation in the broader history of western exploration might suggest. The river drops more than 1,000 feet in its course through Lincoln County, and the rapids and falls that punctuate its channel made navigation a skilled and sometimes dangerous undertaking. The steamboat captains who worked the Kootenai needed to know the river intimately, understanding which channels were navigable at different water levels and which stretches required portaging or lining the boats through with ropes. The men who mastered this knowledge were specialists in a particular kind of river work, and their skills were not transferable to the railroad era that replaced them.

The transition from the steamboat era to the railroad era in the Kootenai country was not simply a change in transportation technology; it was a change in the geography of commerce. The steamboats had concentrated activity at the river's navigable points, creating communities at the places where the river could be crossed or where cargo could be loaded and unloaded. The railroad created a different pattern, running along the valley floor and establishing stations at regular intervals that were determined by the railroad's operational requirements rather than by the natural features of the river. The communities that the railroad created were different in character from the communities that the steamboats had served, and the transition between the two systems left some communities stranded and created others from nothing.

## What Remains Today

The site of Jennings is located in the Kootenai River valley of Lincoln County, along the Great Northern Railway corridor. The Montana Memory Project has documented the community's history, and the Lincoln County Historical Society in Libby maintains records related to the valley's settlement history. The physical evidence of the community's existence depends on its location relative to the Libby Dam reservoir's boundaries.

The Kootenai River in the vicinity of Jennings is part of the landscape that was transformed by the construction of Libby Dam, and the river corridor in this area reflects the managed character of the post-dam Kootenai rather than the natural river that the steamboats navigated.

## Visiting

Jennings is located in Lincoln County, in the Kootenai River valley along the US Highway 2 corridor. Libby, the Lincoln County seat, is the nearest city with visitor services. The Lincoln County Historical Society in Libby can provide information about the history of Jennings and the other communities of the pre-dam Kootenai valley.

Troy is approximately 18 miles west of Libby on US Highway 2 and is the nearest town to the Jennings area.
