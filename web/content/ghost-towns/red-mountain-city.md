# Red Mountain City
Red Mountain City occupied a position in the Highland Mountains of Silver Bow County that was, in almost every respect, secondary to its neighbor Highland City. It was smaller, less documented, and less remembered, and the historical record that survives is thin enough that the town's full story may never be recovered. What is clear is that Red Mountain City was part of the same mining district that produced Highland City, that it drew its population from the same prospecting culture that swept through southwestern Montana in the 1860s, and that it declined and vanished along the same trajectory as the other camps in the Highlands. The red-colored rock formations that gave the town its name are still visible in the mountain terrain south of Butte, a geological marker for a community that left almost no other trace.

## History

The Highland Mountains mining district developed in the years following the major gold discoveries at Bannack and Alder Gulch, as prospectors fanned out from the established camps to test the surrounding ranges. The Highlands, rising south of Butte in Silver Bow County, contained gold and silver deposits that attracted attention in the mid-1860s, and two camps emerged in the district's mountain valleys: Highland City in the larger valley to the west, and Red Mountain City in the terrain to the east, where the oxidized iron minerals in the rock gave the landscape its characteristic color.

Red Mountain City was a smaller camp than Highland City, serving the mines in its immediate vicinity rather than functioning as a district-wide commercial center. The population was never large, and the town's infrastructure was correspondingly modest: a few saloons, a store or two, the boarding houses and cabins that miners built wherever they worked. The camp's fortunes tracked the fortunes of its mines, rising when new ore was found and contracting when the accessible deposits were exhausted.

The relationship between Red Mountain City and Highland City was typical of the pattern seen throughout Montana's mining districts, where a larger commercial center served as the hub of a district that contained several smaller satellite camps. Miners from Red Mountain City would have traveled to Highland City for major supplies, banking, and entertainment, while maintaining their own smaller community near their work. The two towns were close enough that the distinction between them was partly administrative and partly a matter of local identity, the kind of distinction that mattered to the people who lived there but that has largely dissolved in the historical record.

The silver mining that characterized much of the Highland district's later production was a different kind of operation from the placer gold mining that had opened the district. Silver ore required more sophisticated processing than placer gold, and the stamp mills and smelters that handled silver ore were capital-intensive investments that favored larger operators over individual prospectors. The transition from placer gold to silver lode mining in the Highlands, as in most of Montana's mining districts, was a transition from a relatively democratic economy of independent miners to a more hierarchical economy of wage workers and corporate investors. Red Mountain City, as a smaller camp, was more exposed to the volatility of this transition than Highland City, and it declined earlier and more completely.

The collapse of silver prices in 1893, when the United States repealed the Sherman Silver Purchase Act and the price of silver fell by half, was the event that ended the productive life of most of Montana's silver camps. The Highland district was not immune to this shock, and the camps that had depended on silver production were the hardest hit. Red Mountain City, already smaller and less diversified than Highland City, did not survive the crisis as a functioning community.

The broader history of Silver Bow County's mining era is dominated by the story of Butte, which grew from a modest gold camp in the 1860s into the industrial copper capital of the world by the 1880s. The Highland Mountains camps, including Red Mountain City, were part of the district's earlier gold and silver phase, the phase that preceded the copper era and that is now largely overshadowed by Butte's more dramatic story. Recovering the history of Red Mountain City requires looking past the Butte narrative to the smaller, earlier camps that preceded it.

The geological character of the Highland Mountains shaped the kind of mining that was possible there. The iron-oxidized surface formations that gave Red Mountain City its name were the visible expression of a deeper mineralization that contained both gold and silver in varying proportions. The oxidized zone near the surface was the most accessible and the most amenable to the simple processing methods available to early miners, and it was this zone that the first prospectors worked in the 1860s. As mining went deeper, the ore became more complex, requiring more sophisticated processing and greater capital investment. The transition from surface oxidized ore to deeper sulfide material was a challenge that Red Mountain City, like many of Montana's smaller camps, never fully solved.

The men who worked the Highland Mountains camps in the 1860s and 1870s were part of a broader prospecting culture that moved through the American West in the decades following the California gold rush of 1849. They were experienced miners who had worked in California, Nevada, Idaho, and Colorado before coming to Montana, and they brought with them the knowledge and the habits of the mining frontier. Their camps were rough and temporary, built to last only as long as the ore held out, and when the ore was exhausted they moved on to the next discovery, leaving behind the debris of their occupation and the few structures that were too solid to be worth dismantling.

## What Remains Today

Red Mountain City has left almost no visible remains. The site in the Highland Mountains retains the geological character that gave the town its name, with the iron-oxidized red rock formations visible in the mountain terrain. No significant structures from the mining era survive. The site is on national forest land in the Highland Mountains south of Butte. The archaeological work conducted at neighboring Highland City has documented the broader district's history, and some of that documentation touches on the Red Mountain City area.

## Visiting

Red Mountain City is located in the Highland Mountains of Silver Bow County, south of Butte, accessible via forest roads from the Divide area on Interstate 15 or from Butte. The roads are unpaved and require a high-clearance vehicle; conditions vary by season. The site is on national forest land and is open to visitors, though there is little to see beyond the mountain landscape. Butte, the nearest city with full services, is approximately twenty to twenty-five miles to the north and has the World Museum of Mining, which covers the history of Silver Bow County's mining era in detail.
