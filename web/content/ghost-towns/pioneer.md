# Pioneer
Pioneer holds a distinction that is contested in the way that all firsts in Montana history are contested, but the claim is serious and well-documented: it was the site of Montana's first gold camp. The discovery of placer gold on Gold Creek, a tributary of the Clark Fork River in what is now Powell County, was made in 1852 by Francois Finlay, known as Benetsee, a mixed-blood trapper who found float gold in the creek and reported it to the Hudson's Bay Company. The find was not acted on immediately, and it was not until 1862 that a group of prospectors led by John White and James Stuart arrived at Gold Creek and began serious placer mining. Pioneer City grew up to support their work and the work of the men who followed them. It was a genuine town, with hotels, saloons, stores, and a post office, and at its peak it held perhaps five hundred residents. But the gold at Gold Creek was modest compared to what was being found elsewhere, and when the news of richer deposits at Bannack in 1862 and Alder Gulch in 1863 reached Pioneer, the population left almost overnight. The town that had been Montana's first was also among its first to be abandoned.

## History

The story of Pioneer begins with Benetsee's 1852 discovery, but the practical history of the camp starts a decade later. James and Granville Stuart, brothers who would become central figures in early Montana history, were among the first to work the Gold Creek placers seriously in 1862. They found gold, but not in the quantities that would justify a major rush, and the camp they established was modest in scale. John White's discovery on Grasshopper Creek at Bannack in August 1862 drew most of the men away from Gold Creek almost immediately, and the first wave of Pioneer's population departed before the town had fully formed.

The camp did not disappear entirely. The Pioneer Mining District was formally organized in 1866, and the post office opened in 1870. At its peak in the 1870s, Pioneer had four breweries, six saloons, two hotels, and four general stores, a commercial infrastructure that reflected a genuine working population rather than a transient rush camp. The water supply for the placer operations was controlled by a series of ditches, and Conrad Kohrs, the cattle baron who dominated much of central Montana's economy in the post-Civil War years, eventually acquired control of the water rights and used that leverage to buy up mining claims throughout the district. Kohrs's control of the water was the kind of monopoly that shaped the economics of many Montana mining camps, where the man who controlled the water controlled the mining.

Chinese miners were a significant presence at Pioneer, as they were at many of Montana's secondary placer camps. When the white miners moved on in the 1880s as the easily accessible ground played out, Chinese workers came in and worked the tailings, applying the patient and thorough methods that characterized Chinese placer mining across the American West. The University of Montana's research on Chinese miners in Montana notes that Pioneer once had several hundred Chinese miners and was a local hub of commerce for the Chinese community in the region. The Chinese presence at Pioneer lasted longer than the white mining rush, and the camp's extended productive life was partly a result of their work.

The twentieth century brought dredging operations to Gold Creek. Pat Wells, a mining engineer from Butte, recognized the potential for dredging in the area in the late 1920s and bought out the major landholdings, including Kohrs's old water rights. Dredging commenced and continued intermittently through the mid-twentieth century, with the creek being worked three times according to local accounts: once around the turn of the century, once in the early 1930s, and once in the early 1950s after the Korean War. The dredge from the 1930s operations left rusting machinery scattered along the creek; the dredge from the 1950s operations was still intact and sitting in its pond when visitors documented the site in the early 2000s.

The buildings of Pioneer City met an ignominious end. A feud between county authorities and a mining company operating in the area in the later twentieth century resulted in the deliberate destruction of the historic structures. When county officials assessed the buildings as "usable" and levied taxes accordingly, the mining company demolished them rather than pay. A rancher who subsequently owned the property completed the destruction by bulldozing the remaining structures to clear grazing land. The physical record of Montana's first gold camp was erased not by time or weather but by deliberate human action.

## What Remains Today

The Pioneer site today consists of a few ruined log and frame structures, the dredge tailings along Gold Creek, and the rusting machinery from the twentieth-century dredging operations. The miles of rock and gravel piles left by the dredges are the most visible feature of the landscape, giving the Gold Creek valley a distinctive character that reflects the intensity of the mining that occurred there over nearly a century. The 1950s dredge, still sitting in its pond, is a substantial artifact of the later mining era.

The town of Gold Creek, four or five miles north of the Pioneer site, was the supply and social center for the surrounding ranching and mining community throughout the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Families whose ancestors worked the Gold Creek placers still ranch in the valley.

## Visiting

Pioneer is located near Gold Creek in Powell County, accessible via Interstate 90 and secondary roads. The site is on private land, and access has historically depended on the landowner's permission. The Gold Creek valley is open ranch country, and the dredge tailings along the creek are visible from the road. The town of Deer Lodge, the Powell County seat, is approximately twenty miles to the west on Interstate 90 and has the Powell County Museum, which covers the region's mining and ranching history. The Montana Historical Society in Helena holds photographs of Pioneer City from its active years.
