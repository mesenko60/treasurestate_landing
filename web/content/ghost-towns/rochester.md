# Rochester
Rochester sits in a gulch of the Tobacco Root Mountains about eight or nine miles from Twin Bridges, a small camp that owed its existence to the Watseca gold mine and outlasted most of its neighbors by virtue of that mine's unusual longevity. The Watseca was discovered in 1869, and the town of Rochester grew up around it in the years that followed. Where most of Montana's gold camps burned bright and collapsed within a decade, Rochester kept working through the late nineteenth century and into the twentieth, the Watseca mine operating intermittently until the 1980s. What remains today is modest but genuine: a few stone buildings, a wooden head frame, an abandoned gold mill, and the old cemetery that holds the men and women who stayed when others left.

## History

The Rochester district sits in the eastern Tobacco Root Mountains, a range that runs north-south through Madison County and contains some of the more varied mineralization in southwestern Montana. Gold was the primary draw at Rochester, found in the oxidized outcroppings of Rochester Gulch that attracted prospectors in the 1860s. The Watseca mine, discovered in 1869, became the anchor of the district, a lode property that proved more durable than the placer workings that typically defined Montana's first-generation camps.

The town that grew up around the Watseca took the name Rochester, and by the 1870s it had the character of a working mining camp: a store, a saloon or two, a boarding house, and the scattered cabins of miners who preferred to live near their work rather than make the long ride to Twin Bridges or Virginia City. The Hardisty mine was another significant property in the district, contributing to the overall production that made Rochester a recognized name in Madison County mining circles. The camp never achieved the spectacular output of Confederate Gulch or the industrial scale of Granite, but it was a real producer, and the men who worked it were real miners rather than speculators chasing a rumor.

The flooding of the mines was a persistent problem at Rochester, as it was at many of the deeper lode operations in the Tobacco Root Mountains. Water management required pumping equipment and ongoing investment, and when the economics of gold mining shifted against the smaller operators in the late nineteenth century, the cost of keeping the mines dry became a serious obstacle. Some of the mines were abandoned when flooding made them unworkable; others were pumped out and reworked when gold prices rose and new investors arrived.

The twentieth century brought periodic revivals to the Rochester district. The Watseca mine, which had been the original anchor of the camp, continued to attract operators who believed the deeper ground held ore worth extracting. Dredging operations reworked some of the creek gravels, as they did throughout the Tobacco Root range. The mine's operation into the 1980s was a remarkable run for a property first discovered in 1869, a span of more than a century that few Montana mining operations could match.

The social history of Rochester is less well documented than its mining history, but the cemetery tells part of the story. The graves there represent the full range of frontier mortality: accidents in the mines and mills, the diseases that moved through isolated communities, the occasional violence that was never entirely absent from any mining camp. The cemetery has been maintained by local volunteers and is one of the more complete records of Rochester's human history that survives.

The broader Tobacco Root Mountains mining district, of which Rochester was a part, produced gold, silver, copper, and lead from dozens of properties spread across the range. The Rocky Mountain Prospectors Association has documented the Rochester district as part of its survey of Montana placer and lode mining areas, noting the Watseca and Rabbit mining districts as established in the 1860s following the discovery of rich, oxidized gold outcroppings. The total production of the district was modest by the standards of the major Montana camps, but the sustained character of the operation gave Rochester a place in the county's mining history that the flash-and-collapse camps could not claim.

The Tobacco Root Mountains sit between the Madison and Jefferson River valleys, a compact range that rises to over ten thousand feet and contains a geologically complex mix of Precambrian metamorphic rocks intruded by younger igneous bodies. The gold at Rochester occurred in oxidized surface zones above sulfide ore bodies, the kind of deposit that was accessible to early prospectors with simple equipment but that required more sophisticated processing as mining went deeper and the ore became less amenable to direct amalgamation. The transition from surface oxidized ore to deeper sulfide material was a challenge that many of the Tobacco Root camps never fully solved, and it contributed to the periodic abandonment and revival that characterized Rochester's history.

The families who stayed at Rochester through the lean years between mining revivals were ranchers and small farmers who used the valley's water and grass to run cattle and sheep. The Madison Valley's agricultural potential had been recognized from the earliest years of settlement, and the ranching economy that developed alongside the mining economy gave the region a stability that purely mining communities lacked. Rochester's cemetery reflects this mixed heritage, with the graves of miners alongside those of ranchers and their families who made the valley their permanent home.

## What Remains Today

Rochester today retains a few stone buildings from the mining era, a wooden head frame at one of the mine shafts, and an abandoned gold mill that was modern when it was built and is now a ruin. The old cemetery is present and has been maintained. The gulch itself shows the marks of the mining operations, with tailings and disturbed ground visible along the creek. The site is accessible but remote, and the structures that remain are in varying states of deterioration.

## Visiting

Rochester is located in Madison County approximately eight to nine miles from Twin Bridges via secondary roads. The roads into the Tobacco Root Mountains are unpaved and require a high-clearance vehicle; conditions vary significantly by season. The site is on private and public land, and visitors should respect any posted boundaries. Twin Bridges, the Madison County seat, is the nearest service center and has the Madison County Museum. The Tobacco Root Mountains are popular with backcountry travelers and prospectors, and Rochester is one of several historic mining sites accessible from the Twin Bridges area.
