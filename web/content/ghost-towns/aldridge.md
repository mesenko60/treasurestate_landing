# Aldridge
## History

Seven miles northwest of Gardiner, in the mountains that overlook the northern entrance to Yellowstone National Park, the coal camp of Aldridge occupied a position that was both geographically dramatic and economically precarious. The coal veins discovered in the area in 1892 were real and substantial, and the men who developed them built a camp that briefly became one of the more productive coal operations in the country. But the economics of coal mining in a remote mountain location, dependent on expensive transportation and vulnerable to the labor conflicts that characterized the industry in the Progressive Era, were never as favorable as the geology suggested they should be.

The discovery of coal in Park County in 1892 attracted the attention of the Montana Coal and Coke Company, which recognized that the veins near Gardiner could supply fuel for the coke ovens that the smelting industry required. The company moved quickly to develop the property, and by 1895 the mine's main entry had been driven 1,800 feet into the mountain. The workforce that the company assembled was drawn largely from the immigrant communities of southern and eastern Europe, with Austrian and Italian workers predominating. Many of the men who came to Aldridge could not speak English, and the camp developed a multilingual character that set it apart from the Anglo-American mining camps that dominated the Montana landscape.

By 1897 the mines were producing between three hundred and five hundred tons of coal daily for transport to the coke ovens located eight thousand feet away. The scale of the operation was impressive, and the camp that supported it had grown into a substantial community with a union store, a hospital staffed by three doctors, and a social life organized around the rhythms of the immigrant working class. The two most important holidays at Aldridge were Union Day on April 19 and Labor Day, a calendar that reflected the centrality of organized labor to the camp's identity.

The union question came to a head on April 19, 1897, when the Miners' Union organized at Aldridge. The Montana Coal and Coke Company responded by shutting down the mines and the coking plant, refusing to employ union men. The workers voted to stay with the union, and after several months of standoff the company finally accepted a union contract. The victory was a significant one for the immigrant workers who had built the camp, and it established Aldridge as a strong union town for the remainder of its existence. The fortunes of the union thus became the fortunes of the camp, a connection that would prove both a source of strength and a vulnerability.

Progress came to Aldridge in the form of technological improvements that were intended to reduce costs but instead contributed to the camp's decline. The mules that had delivered coal to the coke ovens were replaced first by a flumed water system and later by an expensive electric tramway. The tramway was a significant capital investment, and the Montana Coal and Coke Company financed it by issuing bonds. When the company defaulted on those bonds in 1910, the mines closed despite the richness of the coal veins that remained. The debt that the tramway had created proved more decisive than the ore that the mountain contained.

The closure of the mines in 1910 ended Aldridge's existence as a functioning community. The residents who had built their lives around the coal camp departed as quickly as they had come, moving to other mining districts or to the cities of the Northwest. The buildings they left behind were not substantial enough to resist the mountain climate for long, and the camp dissolved into the landscape with the speed that characterized the end of most company towns.

A related camp called Electric developed near Aldridge during the same period, named for the electric tramway that connected the coal operations to the coke ovens. The two communities were closely linked, sharing the same workforce and the same institutional life, and they are sometimes treated as a single entity in the historical record. The Electric camp eventually merged into Aldridge as the district's operations consolidated, and the combined community was the one that the Montana Coal and Coke Company abandoned when it defaulted on its bonds in 1910.

The story of Aldridge is in many ways the story of the immigrant working class in the American West. The Austrian and Italian miners who built the camp brought with them the traditions of labor organization that they had developed in the industrial districts of Europe, and they applied those traditions to the conditions of a Montana coal camp with considerable success. The union victory of 1897 was a real achievement, and the hospital and union store that the camp maintained were evidence of the community's capacity for collective action. That the camp's end came not from a failure of labor organization but from the financial mismanagement of the company that employed them was an irony that the men who had worked there would have recognized.

## What Remains Today

The Aldridge site retains some structural remnants from its coal mining era, including the foundations of the coke ovens and evidence of the mine workings in the mountains above Gardiner. The site is located on land managed by the Gallatin National Forest and is accessible to visitors interested in the history of coal mining in the Yellowstone region.

The proximity of Aldridge to Yellowstone National Park and to the town of Gardiner makes it a natural addition to any visit to the northern Yellowstone area. The landscape around the site is dramatic, with the mountains of the Gallatin Range providing a backdrop that gives some sense of the physical setting in which the coal camp operated.

## Visiting

Aldridge is located approximately seven miles northwest of Gardiner, Montana, in Park County. The site is accessible via forest roads from Gardiner, which is located at the northern entrance to Yellowstone National Park on US Highway 89. The Gallatin National Forest's Gardiner Ranger District can provide current road and access information.

Gardiner is the nearest town with visitor services and is the gateway community for the northern entrance to Yellowstone National Park. Livingston, the Park County seat, is located approximately 53 miles north on US Highway 89 and Interstate 90.
