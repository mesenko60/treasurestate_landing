# French Gulch
French Gulch holds a distinction that is easy to overlook in the larger story of Montana's gold rush: it was the site of the first gold strike in the Big Hole River watershed. The discovery was made in 1864 by a group of Canadian prospectors who staked placer claims in the gulch and gave it the name it still carries. The community that formed around their work was small and short-lived as a mining camp, but the gulch itself had a longer productive life than the miners who first worked it could have anticipated. When the Anaconda Company arrived in the early twentieth century, it was not for the gold but for the timber, and the logging operations that followed the mining era transformed the gulch's character as thoroughly as the hydraulic sluices had transformed its creek bed. Today French Gulch is a quiet drainage in Deer Lodge County, its mining and logging history largely invisible to the casual traveler, but documented in the records of the Montana Historical Society and the memory of the families whose ancestors worked there.

## History

The Canadian prospectors who named French Gulch in 1864 were part of the broader wave of gold seekers who spread through southwestern Montana in the years following the Bannack and Alder Gulch discoveries. The Big Hole River watershed had not yet been systematically prospected, and the gulch offered the kind of accessible placer ground that attracted experienced miners looking for the next discovery. The gold they found was real but not spectacular, enough to sustain a small camp through the mid-1860s but not enough to trigger the kind of rush that had overwhelmed Bannack and Virginia City.

The community at French Gulch was a working camp rather than a boom town, a place where men came to earn a living from the ground rather than to strike it rich overnight. The New Northwest, the Deer Lodge County newspaper that covered the region's mining activities in the 1870s and 1880s, noted in July 1880 that French Gulch had eighteen white men and fourteen Chinese men working and taking out good money. The presence of Chinese miners at French Gulch was typical of Montana's secondary placer camps, where Chinese workers moved in to rework ground that white miners had abandoned as insufficiently profitable. The patient and thorough methods that characterized Chinese placer mining extended the productive life of many of Montana's smaller camps by years or decades.

William R. Allen, who would later serve as Montana's lieutenant governor from 1909 to 1913, spent his summers at French Gulch working a claim with his father. Allen's presence at the gulch connects the camp to the broader network of Montana's political and business community in the territorial and early statehood era, a reminder that the men who worked these small camps were not isolated from the larger currents of Montana life.

The second chapter of French Gulch's history began when the Anaconda Company recognized the value of the gulch's timber. The dense stands of trees that covered the drainage were exactly what the company needed for mine timbers and for constructing the Anaconda smelters that processed the copper ore from Butte. Gifford Pinchot, the first director of the United States Forest Service and a close ally of President Theodore Roosevelt's conservation program, authorized extensive logging in the gulch. The McClure log flume was built to bring the timber down from the high ground to where it could be transported to the smelters, a piece of industrial infrastructure that transformed the gulch as completely as the mining operations had transformed it before.

The logging era at French Gulch left a different kind of mark on the landscape than the mining era had. Where placer mining disturbed the creek bed and the adjacent ground, logging cleared the hillsides and changed the hydrology of the drainage. The Big Hole Watershed Committee has documented the impacts of both the mining and logging eras on French Gulch and has undertaken restoration work to address the erosion and sedimentation that resulted from a century of intensive land use. The restoration project at French Gulch and Moose Creek is one of the more comprehensive watershed rehabilitation efforts in southwestern Montana.

The Big Hole River watershed is one of the most ecologically significant river systems in Montana, supporting native westslope cutthroat trout, Arctic grayling, and fluvial bull trout in its upper reaches. The disturbance caused by placer mining and hydraulic operations in French Gulch contributed to the sedimentation and habitat degradation that affected fish populations throughout the watershed. The restoration work undertaken by the Big Hole Watershed Committee has focused on stabilizing stream banks, removing legacy mining debris, and restoring riparian vegetation, with the goal of improving water quality and fish habitat in the affected reaches of the drainage.

The Anaconda Company's timber operations at French Gulch were part of a much larger pattern of industrial resource extraction that transformed the landscapes of southwestern Montana in the early twentieth century. The company's need for mine timbers and smelter construction materials drove logging operations across a wide area of the region, and the McClure log flume at French Gulch was one of several such structures built to move timber from the high country to the valley floor. The flume technology was well established in the mining West by the time it was applied at French Gulch, and the engineering required to build and maintain it was a significant undertaking in the steep terrain of the Deer Lodge County mountains.

## What Remains Today

The French Gulch site today shows the marks of both its mining and logging eras. The creek has been the subject of restoration work by the Big Hole Watershed Committee, and the vegetation is recovering from the logging that cleared the hillsides in the early twentieth century. No significant structures from the mining camp era survive. The McClure log flume, which carried timber down from the high ground, has deteriorated beyond recognition. The site is accessible but offers little in the way of visible historical remains; its significance is geological and ecological as much as historical.

## Visiting

French Gulch is located in Deer Lodge County in the Big Hole River watershed, accessible via secondary roads from Anaconda or Wisdom. The roads into the drainage are unpaved and conditions vary by season. The Big Hole Watershed Committee's restoration work has improved public access to parts of the drainage. Anaconda, the nearest service center, is approximately twenty miles to the north and has the Anaconda Visitor Center, which covers the region's copper smelting and mining history. The Pintler Scenic Route connects Anaconda to the Big Hole Valley and passes near the French Gulch drainage.
