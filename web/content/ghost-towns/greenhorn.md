# Greenhorn
The name Greenhorn was applied to mining camps across the American West as a kind of ironic badge, the term for an inexperienced newcomer, worn by the place that attracted them. The Greenhorn Creek camp in Lewis and Clark County, tucked into the Helena National Forest near the town of Austin, was one of those places where men who did not yet know the country came to learn it through the work of placer mining. The gulch and its tributaries, Skelly Gulch and Davis Gulch among them, held placer gold that was worked intermittently from the 1860s through the late 1930s, a span of activity that outlasted most of Montana's more celebrated mining districts precisely because the deposits, while never spectacular, were real and accessible enough to reward patient work. The camp never grew into a town of any consequence, but it left its name on the creek and on the landscape, and the workings it produced are recorded in the files of the Montana Bureau of Mines and Geology.

## History

The Helena area's gold rush began in earnest in 1864, when the discovery at Last Chance Gulch drew thousands of prospectors to what would become the territorial capital. The gulches radiating outward from Helena were explored in the months and years that followed, and Greenhorn Creek, lying to the northwest in what is now the Austin Mining District, was among the drainages that attracted attention. The placer deposits along the creek and its tributaries were not as rich as those at Last Chance or Confederate Gulch, but they were workable, and the men who came to Greenhorn were the kind of miners who preferred steady, modest returns to the boom-and-bust cycle of the major camps.

The Austin Mining District, which encompasses the Greenhorn Creek area, sits within the Helena National Forest at a latitude of approximately 46.66 degrees north, in the foothills country northwest of Helena. The district's geology is characterized by Holocene gravel deposits laid down along creek drainages, with placer gold concentrated in the stream gravels by the same hydraulic processes that had been operating since the last glaciation. The workings along Greenhorn Creek, Skelly Gulch, and Davis Gulch were primarily placer operations, drifting, sluicing, and hydraulic mining of stream and gravel bars, rather than the hard-rock lode mining that characterized the larger districts to the south.

The camp's recorded production history runs from the 1860s through 1939, with the most systematic documentation coming from the Bureau of Mines and Geology surveys of the early twentieth century. The 1939 survey noted that Greenhorn Creek had been worked from a thousand yards below the post office to five hundred yards above it, and that the Evans-Jones Placer, located in the adjacent sections, had been worked in 1942 for rough scheelite concentrate, a tungsten mineral that became strategically important during the Second World War. The presence of scheelite alongside the placer gold reflected the complex mineralogy of the Helena National Forest's mining districts, where the same geological formations that concentrated gold also hosted tungsten and other industrial metals.

The post office that served the Greenhorn Creek community was located in the town of Austin, which functioned as the supply and administrative center for the surrounding mining districts. Austin itself was a small community, but it provided the post office, the general store, and the social infrastructure that the scattered placer miners of the surrounding gulches depended on. The relationship between Austin and Greenhorn Creek was typical of the pattern across Montana's secondary mining districts: a small service town at the valley bottom supporting a dispersed population of miners working the higher ground.

The camp's population was never large and was never stable. Placer mining attracted seasonal workers who came in the spring when the ground thawed and left in the fall when the water froze. The men who worked Greenhorn Creek were not building a permanent community; they were following the gold as far as it went and moving on when it ran out. The fact that the workings continued intermittently for more than seventy years suggests that the deposits were extensive enough to reward repeated attention, even if no single season produced a fortune.

The Chinese miners who worked many of Montana's secondary placer districts were likely present at Greenhorn Creek as well, though the historical record is sparse. Across the Helena district, Chinese placer miners reworked ground that white miners had abandoned as unprofitable, applying patient and thorough methods to gravels that had already been sluiced once. The Helena National Forest's records of Chinese mining activity in the broader area suggest that Greenhorn Creek's long productive life, stretching from the 1860s into the 1930s, was partly the result of this successive reworking. The creek's modest but persistent yields were the kind that rewarded thoroughness over speed.

The wartime scheelite operation at the Evans-Jones Placer represented a different kind of mining entirely. Scheelite, a calcium tungstate mineral, was essential for hardening steel used in armor plate and cutting tools, and the federal government's wartime demand for tungsten sent prospectors back to old mining districts across the West in search of deposits that had been overlooked or dismissed during the silver and gold years. The Evans-Jones Placer's scheelite was extracted in 1942, a single wartime season that connected the old placer camp to the industrial demands of a global conflict.

## What Remains Today

The Greenhorn Creek area is today part of the Helena National Forest, administered by the Helena-Lewis and Clark National Forest. The old placer workings are visible along the creek and its tributaries as disturbed ground, gravel bars, and the remnants of sluice box operations. The Evans-Jones Placer site, where scheelite was extracted during the 1940s, is identifiable in the landscape by the surface disturbance associated with its wartime operation. No standing structures from the mining era remain at the site.

The broader Austin Mining District retains several other historic mining properties, and the area is popular with recreational gold panners who work the same creek gravels that the nineteenth-century placer miners exploited. The Helena National Forest manages the area for multiple uses, and recreational prospecting is permitted in designated areas under applicable regulations.

## Visiting

Greenhorn Creek is located approximately three miles from the town of Austin in Lewis and Clark County, accessible via Forest Road 187 and secondary roads within the Helena National Forest. Austin is reached from Helena via Highway 12 west, then north on secondary roads. The area is open to visitors throughout the summer and fall, with the creek accessible for hiking and recreational gold panning. No developed facilities exist at the historic mining sites. The Helena-Lewis and Clark National Forest Ranger District can provide current access information and regulations for recreational prospecting in the area.
