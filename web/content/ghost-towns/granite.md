# Granite
## History

The mill whistle at Granite sounded the end of a world. It blew on a day in August 1893, and when it stopped, the men working the Granite Mountain Mine and the Bimetallic Mine put down their tools, collected what they could carry, and walked out of the mountain. Within a year, the population of what had been called the richest silver town on earth fell from thirty-two hundred to one hundred and forty. The buildings remained. The machinery remained. The ore in the mountain remained. Only the people left, and they left because Congress had repealed the Sherman Silver Purchase Act, and the price of silver had fallen below the cost of extracting it, and there was nothing more to be done.

The story of Granite begins, as so many Montana mining stories do, with a prospector and a piece of rock. In the autumn of 1872, a man named Eli D. Holland was working his way across Granite Mountain in the Flint Creek Range, approximately five miles southeast of the small settlement of Philipsburg, when he identified high-quality ruby silver ore in the rock face and sank a shallow test shaft to assess the vein. The ore was rich, but Holland lacked the capital to develop it, and the claim was abandoned. It sat untouched for nearly a decade.

The revival came in 1880, when Charles D. McLure, the superintendent of the nearby Hope Mill in Philipsburg, rediscovered the abandoned shaft and recovered a specimen of ruby silver from the dump. The specimen assayed at two thousand ounces of silver per ton, a figure so extraordinary that McLure immediately understood what he had found. He secured financing, partnered with a man named Charles Clark, and formed the Granite Mountain Mining Company, capitalized at ten million dollars by investors in St. Louis. The company town of Granite was platted on the steep slopes of the mountain in 1884, and the work of building one of the great silver operations of the nineteenth century began in earnest.

The town that grew up around the mine was a company town in the fullest sense: the land was owned by the company, the lots were rented to miners for two dollars and fifty cents a month, and the rhythms of daily life were organized around the rhythms of the mine. But within those constraints, Granite developed a genuine community, one shaped by the diverse origins of its workforce. Finnish miners settled along what became known as Finnlander Lane. Irish miners clustered in Donegal Row. Cornish, Danish, and Chinese workers occupied their own corners of the townsite, each group bringing its own customs, foods, and social organizations to the mountain. By 1889, the town had four churches, a school, eighteen saloons, a hospital, a three-story Miners' Union Hall, and a four-mile bob sled run down to Philipsburg that served as both winter recreation and, in a pinch, a rapid means of transport.

The mine itself was producing at a scale that justified all of it. The Granite Mountain Mine struck what geologists called the Bonanza Chute in 1882, a body of ore assaying at seventeen hundred ounces of silver per ton. The initial shipments from the Bonanza Chute generated two hundred and seventy-four thousand dollars. Under the management of John W. Plummer, who took over in 1885, the operation expanded from a twenty-stamp mill to an eighty-stamp mill, and production climbed from two and a half million dollars between 1885 and 1888 to four million dollars annually by 1890. The adjacent Bimetallic Mine, formed in 1886, added a hundred-stamp mill and an eight-thousand-eight-hundred-and-fifty-foot drainage tunnel to the district's infrastructure. From 1883 to 1893, the Granite and Bimetallic mines together produced more than twenty-six million dollars in bullion and paid thirteen million dollars in dividends to their investors. The district total reached thirty-nine million dollars by 1913.

Granite at its peak was a place of real consequence, not merely a camp but a town, with a population of three thousand people living at seven thousand feet in the mountains of western Montana, connected to the outside world by a railroad extension and sustained by ore that seemed, in those years, inexhaustible. The men who worked the mines came from across Europe and from China, drawn by wages that were better than what they could earn elsewhere and by the particular democracy of the mining camp, where a man's worth was measured by his capacity for hard work rather than his origins. The Miners' Union Hall, three stories of dressed stone and timber, stood as evidence that the workers understood their collective power and were prepared to exercise it.

Then came 1893. The repeal of the Sherman Silver Purchase Act, which had required the federal government to purchase a fixed quantity of silver each month, removed the floor from the silver market. Prices fell by nearly half almost overnight. The Granite Mountain Mine and the Bimetallic Mine were profitable at one price and unprofitable at another, and the new price was the wrong one. The mill whistle sounded the end, and the men walked out, and the town that had taken a decade to build emptied in a matter of months.

Limited mining resumed in 1898, after consolidation of the district's claims, and the revived operation produced about a million dollars annually until 1901. But the population never recovered, and the infrastructure that had supported three thousand people was not rebuilt for the few hundred who returned. Mining continued intermittently through the 1930s, sustained by occasional rises in silver prices and the stubborn optimism of men who believed the mountain still had something to give. A major fire in 1958 destroyed most of the remaining surface structures. The last permanent resident of Granite, a woman named Mae Werning, died in 1969, and the site became a state park.

## What Remains Today

Granite Ghost Town State Park preserves a small but significant portion of the original townsite, including the ruins of the mine superintendent's house and the stone walls of the Miners' Union Hall. The park encompasses only about two-tenths of an acre, making it one of the smallest state parks in Montana, but the ruins within it are substantial and evocative. The stone walls of the Union Hall rise several stories above the mountain slope, their windows open to the sky, the interior floors long since collapsed.

Additional ruins are scattered across the broader townsite, which extends beyond the park boundary. The scale of what was once here is apparent in the foundations, the tailings piles, and the remnants of the tramway system that connected the mine to the mill. The mountain setting, at seven thousand feet in the Flint Creek Range, is spectacular in any season.

## Visiting

Granite Ghost Town State Park is located approximately five miles southeast of Philipsburg, Granite County. From Philipsburg, take Montana Highway 1 south and then turn onto Granite Road, a four-mile gravel road that climbs steeply to the townsite. The road requires a vehicle with high clearance; four-wheel drive is recommended, particularly in wet conditions. The park is typically open from May 1 through September 30, daylight hours only. No admission fee is charged.

The elevation and mountain location mean that weather can change rapidly. Snow is possible in any month. Visitors should bring water, as no services are available at the site.
