# Surrounded by Wilderness

**By Editor**

*Hungry Horse, Flathead County, Montana, March 2017*

When a man stands on the edge of the Upper Flathead River, he is looking at a piece of the world that has stubbornly refused to be improved by civilization. The river drains Glacier National Park, the Bob Marshall Wilderness Complex, and the southeastern corner of British Columbia, pouring out cold, clear water that has never seen the inside of a factory pipe. In 1976, the federal government looked at 219 miles of the three forks of the Flathead and decided they were wild and scenic enough to be left exactly as they were, managing them to maintain their natural primitive environments and unpolluted waters. It is a rare thing for a government to look at a river and decide the best thing to do is absolutely nothing.

Directly to the south of this spot lies the Swan Mountain Range, stretching in an unbroken line for a hundred miles without a single road crossing its top, a fact that must surely frustrate the men who sell asphalt. East of the Swan Range is "the Bob," a million-acre expanse of timber and rock where the only way in is on foot or horseback, and the only way out is the same. Just around the next corner going toward the park, you can look east into the Great Bear Wilderness, created in 1978 to provide vital habitat for the grizzly bear and other wildlife that prefer their neighbors to be at least a mountain range away. It is a vast, quiet country, and a man could walk into it and not be heard from again until he was good and ready.
