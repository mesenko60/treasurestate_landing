# Great Northern Railway Passenger and Freight Depot and Division Office

**By Mark Twain**

*Whitefish, Flathead County, Montana, 1928*

Whitefish served as a division point for the Great Northern Railway from its founding in 1904 until 1955. In 1925, one railroader called it "the most distinctively railroad town on the whole Great Northern system," a compliment that might have been taken as an insult by a town less dependent on the iron horse. The second floor of this 1928 Tudor style building, designed by railroad architect Thomas McMahon, housed the railway's division offices. It was a place where men in green eyeshades tallied the cost of moving a continent's worth of goods, and where the click of the telegraph key was the heartbeat of the enterprise.

Serving as a hub for passenger and freight transportation, the first floor housed the yard office, freight and baggage rooms, warm room, ticket office, general waiting room, telegraph office, men's smoking room, and ladies' rest room. It was a microcosm of society, where the wealthy traveler in a Pullman car might brush shoulders with a logger seeking a ticket to the next camp. Competition from automobiles and trucks had already begun to decrease railroad traffic, so not many depots were built in the 1920s, the height of Tudor popularity. Thus, Tudor style depots are rare, a fact that the Great Northern Railway no doubt pointed out to anyone who would listen.

This depot's Tudor features include its high pointed roof, stucco and decorative half timbering above clapboard, and multiple groups of tall, narrow windows. Its decoratively carved brackets and rafter tails and second-floor balconies echo similar detailing at Glacier National Park chalets, visually connecting Whitefish to Glacier, a tourist destination widely promoted by the railroad. It was a clever piece of marketing, suggesting that the romance of the mountains began the moment one stepped off the train.

In the 1980s, Whitefish preservationists worked to preserve this magnificent building, which still serves as a passenger and freight depot. Railroad depots are one of the few types of buildings for which the back, facing the town, is as important as the front, facing the tracks. The stylish façades on both front and back of the Whitefish Depot continue to welcome travelers and reflect the town's railroad history, a history that is written in steel and steam, and in the memories of those who rode the rails.
