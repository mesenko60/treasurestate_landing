# Atlantic Cable Quartz Lode

In June 1867, three prospectors named Alexander Aiken, John Person, and Jonas Stough lost their horses near Flint Creek and stumbled upon a gold quartz lode so rich it defied belief. They named it the Atlantic Cable Quartz Lode, a nod to the recent laying of the second transatlantic telegraph cable, and soon a small camp called Cable City sprang up around the find. The Helena Weekly Herald declared the men of this extraordinary camp to be of "extended experience, good hard sense and big hearts," though the mine itself operated with indifferent success for over a decade.

It wasn't until 1880 that miners struck a vein dead under the mountain that would eventually produce over one hundred and fifty million dollars in modern money. Butte copper king William A. Clark even paid ten thousand dollars for a single chunk of ore taken from this mine in 1889, claiming it was the largest gold nugget ever found in Montana. The mine and its nearby Gold Coin Mill operated sporadically until 1940, leaving behind a legacy of accidental discovery and staggering wealth.
