# Nez Perce War

**By editor**

*Fort Benton, Chouteau County, Montana*

In the waning days of summer 1877, the Nez Perce, a tribe long accustomed to the rugged landscapes of the Pacific Northwest, undertook a desperate flight across the northern plains. Their journey, beginning in their homelands in Idaho and culminating near the Canadian border, was a poignant episode in the annals of American expansion and indigenous resistance. Fort Benton, situated on the upper Missouri River in Montana Territory, found itself drawn into this drama, albeit in a role of limited military consequence but significant symbolic import.

The Nez Perce War arose from the mounting tensions provoked by the United States government’s persistent efforts to confine Native American tribes to reservations, often in violation of prior treaties. The Nez Perce bands, under the leadership of chiefs such as Joseph, Looking Glass, and White Bird, sought to avoid the fate of dispossession that had befallen many tribes before them. When conflict erupted in June 1877, the Nez Perce chose flight over submission, embarking on a remarkable retreat covering more than a thousand miles.

By September of that year, the Nez Perce had crossed into the Judith Basin of central Montana. Here, Major Guido Ilges, the commandant at Fort Benton, acted upon reports of the tribe’s approach and the likelihood of their crossing the Missouri River. Major Ilges, a figure entrusted with the defense of the remote outpost, swiftly organized a detachment of fifty volunteers drawn chiefly from the Irish Fenian community residing in Fort Benton. These men, seasoned by their own struggles and imbued with a martial spirit, were tasked with intercepting the Nez Perce as they sought passage across the river.

In a display of logistical ingenuity, the volunteers loaded a mountain howitzer, a light artillery piece capable of firing explosive shells, onto a mackinaw boat--a flat-bottomed vessel suited for the shallow Missouri--while the remainder of the party took to horseback. Their objective was twofold: to protect Fort Claggett, a modest military installation some miles downstream, and to safeguard the freight at the Cow Island steamboat landing, a vital node in the flow of goods and communication along the river.

Despite their haste, the volunteers arrived too late to prevent the Nez Perce from crossing the Missouri at Cow Island. The tribe, numbering nearly eight hundred souls--comprising warriors, women, and children--had already begun their passage, undeterred by the military presence. The volunteers pursued the fleeing bands along what became known as the Cow Island Trail, a rugged path that led into the more forbidding terrain of the Bear Paw Mountains.

The encounter that followed proved far more perilous than anticipated. The Nez Perce, experienced in warfare and driven by the imperative of survival, resisted with determined ferocity. The volunteers, unprepared for the scale and resolve of the opposition, suffered casualties--one man killed and another wounded in the skirmish. Recognizing the futility of pressing their advantage against a larger and more agile force, the volunteers prudently withdrew to the safety of the steamboat landing, where they awaited the arrival of the United States Army.

This episode at Cow Island may appear a minor footnote amid the grand movements of the Nez Perce War, yet it underscores the challenges faced by the military and settlers in contesting the movements of a mobile and resolute indigenous force. Fort Benton’s involvement, though limited in scope, reflects the strategic importance of the Missouri River corridor as both a conduit for commerce and a frontier of conflict.

The ultimate conclusion of the Nez Perce flight occurred in early October 1877, when the U.S. Army, led by General Oliver O. Howard and Colonel Nelson A. Miles, finally overtook the tribe at Snake Creek in the Bear Paw Mountains of Montana. After a grueling battle that lasted several days under the harsh conditions of approaching winter, Chief Joseph, the foremost leader of the Nez Perce bands, surrendered. His words at that moment have since resonated through history: "From where the sun now stands, I will fight no more forever."

These words encapsulate the profound exhaustion and sorrow that marked the end of the Nez Perce’s valiant but ultimately unsuccessful resistance. The tribe had traversed an arduous route of 1,179 miles, evading and engaging the army through the rugged landscapes of Idaho, the Yellowstone region, and into Montana, drawing near the international boundary with Canada--a distance and feat unparalleled in the annals of Native American warfare.

Fort Benton, though not the site of the final confrontation, felt the reverberations of the war’s conclusion. The town, once a bustling hub of river traffic and trade, received the returning troops after the surrender, a somber procession that marked the closing of this chapter of frontier conflict. The presence of Major Ilges and his volunteers at Cow Island, though ultimately unable to halt the Nez Perce passage, was emblematic of the wider network of military and civilian efforts to impose order upon the vast and contested territories of the American West.

The Nez Perce War must be understood within the larger context of the United States’ expansionist policies in the late nineteenth century. The government’s insistence on relocating tribes to reservations, often on lands less suited to their traditional ways of life, fomented resistance and tragedy. General Howard, who pursued the Nez Perce relentlessly, was a seasoned officer with a career shaped by Indian wars. His correspondence reveals a complex view of the campaign, acknowledging both the tactical difficulties and the human costs involved.

In a letter to the Secretary of War dated November 1877, Howard wrote: "The Nez Perce have shown a degree of skill and endurance in their retreat that commands respect; yet their surrender has brought an end to a conflict which could not be prolonged without leading to greater loss on both sides." This reflection highlights the tragic impasse of a conflict driven by inexorable forces of settlement and survival.

The Nez Perce War, with its dramatic narrative of pursuit, evasion, and final capitulation, remains a pivotal episode in the history of the American frontier. Fort Benton’s role, though modest, is a reminder of the many localities across this vast region that found themselves entwined in the fate of peoples struggling to maintain their place upon the land. The story of Major Ilges and his volunteers at Cow Island, caught between the ambitions of empire and the resolve of a native people, is a chapter worthy of remembrance.
