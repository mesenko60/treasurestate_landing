# First Presbyterian Church of Whitefish

**By Mark Twain**

*Whitefish, Flathead County, Montana, 1903*

Not long after the Great Northern Railway announced its plans for a division point in Whitefish, Presbyterian missionary E. M. Ellis and Kalispell minister Alexander Pringle traveled by bicycle and rowboat to visit the site. It is a testament to their zeal that they undertook such a journey, for the roads were likely no better than the river, and the river was likely full of snags. Soon after, Reverend Pringle canvassed logging and railroad camps for donations of cash and labor to construct a sanctuary. By December 1903, Whitefish had its first church, a modest affair that served its purpose until the congregation outgrew it.

The First Presbyterian Church moved several times in the early years, like a restless spirit seeking a permanent home. By 1919, it had once again outgrown its building. To accommodate congregants, the church held services in the Masonic Lodge while planning a new house of worship. Under the direction of physician and active church member W. W. Taylor, the building committee devised detailed drawings, which the Spokane architectural firm Rigg and Vantyne modified only slightly. They chose a Romanesque Revival style design, considered less ostentatious and more appropriate for a Protestant church than the competing Gothic tradition, which was perhaps too suggestive of popery for their tastes.

Romanesque Revival churches featured masonry construction, heavily arched windows, bands of stylized decoration, and towers. In this case, a Norman style square tower serves as the building's main entrance, looking sturdy enough to withstand a siege, or at least a very strong wind. The one-story building featured a large daylight basement with a high ceiling, designed to provide clearance for a full-sized basketball court. It is a practical addition, for the spirit must be nourished, but the body must also be exercised, especially in the long Montana winters.

Community members donated the large art glass windows ornamenting the sanctuary. Among them are two purchased by Japanese railroad workers for $700 in honor of churchwoman Elizabeth Peck, who taught the men English. A tribute to Peck, the windows also commemorate Whitefish's once-thriving Japanese community and the church's long history of community service. It is a fine thing when a church remembers that its duty is not only to the next world, but to this one as well.
