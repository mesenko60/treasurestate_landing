# Cow Island Incident

**By editor**

*Fort Benton, Chouteau County, Montana*

In the waning days of September 1877, the Missouri River bore witness to a moment fraught with the desperation and turmoil that had engulfed the American West for many years. On the 21st of that month, Major Guido Ilges, commanding officer at the strategic frontier post of Fort Benton, received urgent intelligence that the Nimíipuu, commonly known as the Nez Perce, had pressed through the Judith Basin, advancing northward toward the sanctuary of Canada. The Nez Perce, a people of profound endurance and resolve, were then engaged in a flight from the relentless pursuit of the United States Army, seeking to evade subjugation and preserve their freedom.

Major Ilges acted swiftly. He marshaled thirteen soldiers of Company F, Seventh Infantry Regiment, and enlisted two civilian volunteers to man a steamboat on the Missouri River. Upon its deck was placed a mountain howitzer, a weapon of considerable firepower intended to defend the vital supply depot at the Cow Island steamboat landing and the adjacent Fort Claggett. Alongside the vessel rode thirty-eight mounted volunteers and one soldier, their horses urging them to keep pace with the river flotilla. Their mission was clear: to safeguard the supplies critical to sustaining the army’s operations in this remote and contested region.

Yet, their efforts proved too late. As the steamboat approached Cow Island, smoke and flames smoldering upon the horizon revealed the grim reality. A contingent of the Nimíipuu had intercepted an army supply wagon near Cow Creek, appropriating the provisions necessary for their survival and setting the remainder ablaze. Overwhelmed by the swift and resolute action of the Nez Perce, Major Ilges found his forces outnumbered and unable to secure the depot. With prudence born of experience, he resolved to withdraw, returning to the relative safety of Fort Benton.

The incident at Cow Island is but a single episode in the greater saga of the Nez Perce War, a conflict born of tragic misunderstanding and inexorable expansion. The summer of 1877 had proved long and bitter for the Nimíipuu. In June of that year, the United States government had issued an ultimatum demanding their removal from their ancestral lands in northeastern Oregon and western Idaho to the confines of a reservation. Nearly eight hundred Nez Perce men, women, and children, unwilling to submit to this forced exile, embarked upon a journey remarkable in its scope and endurance. Their path would span 1,179 miles, traversing the rugged wilds of Idaho, the volcanic wilderness of Yellowstone, and the vast plains of Montana.

This flight was not a mere retreat but a desperate endeavor to preserve their way of life. The United States Army, determined to enforce federal policy and quell what it deemed insurrection, pursued the Nez Perce relentlessly. Among the army’s commanders was Colonel Nelson A. Miles, a seasoned officer tasked with bringing the conflict to a close. His troops finally overtook the Nez Perce at the Bear Paw Mountains in Montana, a mere forty miles from the safety of the Canadian border.

After enduring five harrowing days marked by bitter cold and starvation, Chief Joseph, the revered leader of the Nez Perce, recognized the futility of further resistance. On October 5, 1877, amid the snow-covered plains, he surrendered. The words he spoke upon relinquishing his rifle have since entered the annals of American history: "From where the sun now stands, I will fight no more forever." This declaration was not merely the capitulation of a defeated warrior but the poignant acknowledgment of a people brought low by the vast forces arrayed against them.

The fate of the Nez Perce following their surrender was harsh. Those who remained with Chief Joseph were exiled to Indian Territory, present-day Oklahoma, a landscape far removed from their Pacific Northwest homeland. It was only after eight years of hardship and displacement that survivors were permitted to return to reservations within the Northwest. Not all Nez Perce surrendered; Chief White Bird, a stalwart leader who had escaped with the able-bodied warriors, crossed into Canada to evade capture. There, in exile, he lived out the remainder of his days.

The strategic importance of Cow Island and its steamboat landing cannot be understated. Situated along the Missouri River, it functioned as a critical node for military logistics and supply in a region where transportation was otherwise arduous and slow. Control of such points meant sustaining or starving the forces on the frontier. The Nez Perce, understanding this, targeted the supply wagon near Cow Creek deliberately, undermining the army’s ability to maintain its presence and pursue them effectively. This action was emblematic of the resourcefulness and tactical acumen the Nez Perce displayed throughout their flight.

Major Ilges’ decision to retreat in the face of overwhelming numbers was consistent with the military prudence of the time. He recognized that without sufficient force, attempting to secure Cow Island would result in unnecessary loss. His orders were to protect, not to provoke an engagement that could jeopardize the entire garrison at Fort Benton. The Cow Island Incident thus illustrates the complex interplay of military necessity and the harsh realities on the frontier, where soldiers often found themselves outnumbered and outmaneuvered by indigenous warriors intimately familiar with the terrain.

The Nez Perce War, culminating in the Cow Island Incident and the subsequent Bear Paw surrender, encapsulates the tragic collision between the expanding United States and the native peoples who sought to maintain their sovereignty. It is a chapter marked by courage and suffering on both sides, reflective of the broader struggles that shaped the American West.

Colonel Nelson Miles, reflecting on the campaign, remarked in his official report: "The Nez Perce are the most intelligent and determined Indians I have ever encountered, and their retreat was marked by skill and endurance seldom surpassed." His words acknowledge the respect earned by the Nimíipuu even in defeat, a recognition of their indomitable spirit against overwhelming odds.

As the smoke cleared over Cow Island and the Nez Perce vanished toward the Canadian border, the frontier moved inexorably toward a new order, one in which indigenous autonomy was increasingly constrained. Yet, the memory of these struggles endures in the landscape and in the history of Montana, a solemn chapter in the saga of a continent in transformation.
