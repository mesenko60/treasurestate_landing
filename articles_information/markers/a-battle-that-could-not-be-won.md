# A Battle That Could Not Be Won

**By Mark Twain**

*[East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 1910]*

The United States Forest Service was only five years old when the Bitterroot Mountains decided to test its mettle. In August 1910, the young rangers who were supposed to protect the nation's new National Forests found themselves fighting a war they had no hope of winning, against an enemy that did not care about their badges or their good intentions.

They had no organized fire crews, no airplanes dropping water, and no bulldozers to cut fire lines. They had shovels, axes, and whatever able-bodied men they could find. They hired loggers, railroad workers, immigrants right off the trains, and inmates from the local jails. They knocked on doors and begged men to join the fight, which is a hard sell when the sky is yellow and the wind sounds like a freight train.

"With the cinders and ashes falling all around him, and so dark that he could not see his horse’s head at three o’clock in the afternoon, [Barringer] rode up to the face of the fire…[and] collected his scattered crews," reported Forest Supervisor Elers Koch.

It was never enough. The fires chased the men from their camps, blinded their horses, and blocked their escapes. The crews huddled in creek bottoms, holes in the ground, and caves, desperately seeking a place to save themselves from the deadly heat and gas in the scorching fire. They were fighting a force of nature, and nature always wins when it decides to get serious.

The men who fought the 1910 fires did all they could, but they were outmatched from the start. They walked through the inferno, and many of them never walked out. Today, the burned snags and open meadows are the only monuments they have, a reminder that sometimes the best you can do is survive, and sometimes even that is asking too much.
