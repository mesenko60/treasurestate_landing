# St. Paul Pass Tunnel

**By Editor**

*East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 2011*

When the Chicago, Milwaukee and St. Paul Railroad decided to punch a hole through the Bitterroot Mountains, they didn't do it by halves. They set out to drill a tunnel twenty-three feet high, sixteen feet wide, and 1.7 miles long, straight into Idaho. It was a damp, dark, dirty dig, the kind of job that makes a man reconsider his life choices. After a faltering start in 1907, work began in earnest in 1908, with crews toiling around the clock in wet, miserable conditions. At their absolute best, they could manage twenty feet a day, which is about the speed of a particularly determined snail.

The company official who remembered that "Men were hard to keep as the work was disagreeable and hard" was guilty of the understatement of the century. Several large veins of water were encountered, and at times the working conditions were almost unbearable. It took 750 men - 400 tunneling inside, 200 outside removing the dirt and rock, and 150 running the dig's power plant yards - two and a half years to complete the job. They set up a steam-driven electric power plant four miles away in Taft, Montana, to power both ends of the dig, using compressed air to run the giant steam shovels that loaded the blasted rock into electric rail cars. When the first ladies went through the tunnel in February 1909, they had to wear "sou'westers" and slickers, because water was still pouring out of the ceiling at up to 1,500 gallons a minute. It was a triumph of modern engineering, assuming your idea of a triumph involves standing in a freezing underground waterfall for two years.
