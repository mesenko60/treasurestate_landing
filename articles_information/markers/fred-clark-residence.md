# Fred Clark Residence

Anaconda's elite began to build homes on the West Side in the 1890s, putting as much distance as possible between their front porches and the sulfurous breath of Marcus Daly's smelter. The idyllic neighborhood, with its tree-lined sidewalks, street lamps, and large public park, attracted the town's upper and middle-class families. Among the first to grace upper Locust Street was this Queen Anne style mansion, built in 1894 for Fred Clark, the librarian at the Hearst Free Public Library.

The elegant residence boasted all the latest conveniences, including steam heat and electric lights, which were no small luxury in a town that had been a hodgepodge of shacks just a decade earlier. Classical elements such as Tuscan columns and a gabled portico enrich the Queen Anne style so favored during the Victorian era. An octagonal tower, gabled dormers, and a wraparound veranda with a balconette above heighten the characteristic asymmetry, while granite-capped windows and decorative shingles add varied textures to the wall surfaces. Arched doors and windows showcase the skills of builder and contractor J. P. Dolan, who, along with his partner John Hamill, was one of Anaconda's leading contractors in the 1890s.

The house was built during a period of intense cultural ambition in Anaconda, largely funded by Phoebe Hearst, wife of wealthy California senator George Hearst. Mrs. Hearst had a special relationship with the town, as her husband was one of Daly's principal investors. She took an active interest in improving Anaconda's cultural opportunities, asking Clark to manage the library she gifted to the city. The library was a magnificent classical structure that the *Anaconda Standard* described on June 12, 1898, as having "not its equal in Montana; it will compare favorably with any public building in the West."

Except for a 1990 addition sensitively designed to blend with the historic appearance, this West Side residence remains a district centerpiece and one of Anaconda's many well-preserved period homes. It stands as a quiet monument to the days when copper kings and their librarians built mansions in the shadow of the world's largest smokestack.
