# Flathead House

**By Mark Twain**

*[Plains, Sanders County, Montana, November 1824]*

By the second decade of the nineteenth century, the Hudson's Bay Company had established a very comfortable monopoly in northwestern Montana, trading blankets and beads for the furs of the Kootenai, Pend d'Oreille, and Salish people. In 1812, they built a trading post near the mouth of the Thompson River, and by the early 1820s, they relocated it to a more accessible spot near present-day Plains. They called it Flathead House, which consisted of two dwellings, a store, and a horse corral. It was a tidy, profitable arrangement, entirely free of competition, which is exactly how a monopoly prefers to operate.

Then, in November 1824, Jedediah Smith and seven American companions arrived uninvited.

The Hudson's Bay Company, under the direction of John McLoughlin, had spent years trying to keep American trappers out of the Oregon country west of the Continental Divide. Their strategy was simple and ruthless: trap every beaver in western Montana so the Americans would have nothing to catch and would therefore stay home. It was a policy of deliberate extermination, executed with British efficiency.

When Smith showed up at the remote trading post, company manager Peter Skene Ogden was not pleased. He recorded the event in his journal, calling it "that damn'd all cursed day." Ogden recognized immediately that the arrival of the Americans signaled the end of the Hudson's Bay Company's exclusive control over the region. The Americans were not there to trade; they were there to trap, and they were entirely indifferent to British claims of sovereignty.

The establishment of the 49th parallel as the international boundary in 1846 finally forced the British to close Flathead House in 1847. But the real end of the monopoly began on that cursed day in 1824, when Jedediah Smith walked out of the wilderness and ruined Peter Skene Ogden's perfectly good monopoly.
