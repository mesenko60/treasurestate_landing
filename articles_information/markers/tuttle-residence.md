# Tuttle Residence

Mass production of decorative details allowed even modest houses to partake of architectural fashion in the 1890s, when Anaconda's elite began to build homes on the West Side. In the case of this one-and-one-half-story home, stained glass, gingerbread and latticework, turned porch supports, and fish-scale shingles in the gable end visually reference the popular Queen Anne style. Although Charles Tuttle did not formally acquire the land on which this home was built until 1897, he had contractors Smith and Gilmour construct the wood frame residence in 1892, making this one of the oldest homes on Hickory Street.

Tuttle, born in New Hampshire in 1860, arrived in Montana in 1888 and became a payroll clerk for the Anaconda Copper Mining Company. By 1890, he had started his own business, the City Drug Co., where he sold pharmaceuticals, hardware, and furniture. In the 1890s, a large furniture warehouse stood behind the home. In 1898, Tuttle expanded his business to include a funeral parlor, selling the drug store in 1900 to focus on undertaking. He later added a livery, creating Tuttle's Livery and Undertaking.

Longtime resident Joseph Malloy, a foreman at the converter plant, and his wife, Sarah, purchased the residence in 1915. More recently, James Milo and Lenore Manning undid alterations from the 1950s and 1960s, restoring the front façade to its original appearance. The home remains a quiet monument to the days when a man could arrive in a copper camp as a clerk and end up as the town's leading undertaker, living in a gingerbread house on the West Side.
