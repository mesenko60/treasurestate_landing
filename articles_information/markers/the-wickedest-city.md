# The Wickedest City

**By Editor**

*East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 2011*

When the Chicago, Milwaukee and St. Paul Railroad decided to bore a 1.7-mile hole through the Bitterroot Mountains, they needed a place to put the men who were doing the boring. What sprouted up in the valley below was a "den of iniquity" so thoroughly dedicated to vice that the *Chicago Tribune* in 1909 bestowed upon it the proud title of "the wickedest city in America." They called it Taft, presumably because naming it after the President of the United States added a nice touch of irony to a town that consisted of twenty-seven saloons, two theaters that were mostly saloons, a hospital, a hotel, and a few legitimate businesses that probably felt very out of place.

No one ever took an accurate census of Taft, mostly because the census takers were likely afraid to get off the train. One source estimated the population at about a thousand inhabitants, noting that "Women of the underworld, gamblers, etc., flocked to the 'mushroom' railroad town." The women, popularly known as "canaries," were a diverse collection of entrepreneurs. There was Dago Red, famous for her extraordinary stamina, and Pee Wee Jack, a 250-pound woman who owned a parrot trained to ask, "Do you want to go to bed?" Murder was so common that every spring, half a dozen bodies melted out of the snow behind the bars and brothels, reaching a toll of at least 44 by 1910. The town survived near riots, panics, floods, and a 1908 fire, only to be mostly wiped out by the great 1910 fire. Sixty years later, bulldozers finally flattened what was left to make way for Interstate 90, burying the bones of the wickedest city under a layer of respectable asphalt.
