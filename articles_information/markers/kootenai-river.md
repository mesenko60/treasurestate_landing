# Kootenai River

**By Mark Twain**

*Troy, Lincoln County, Montana, 1809*

The river is named for the Kootenai tribe that lived and hunted in this part of Montana and adjoining territory in Idaho and Canada. They were settled south of Flathead Lake in 1855 with the Salish on the Flathead Reservation, a piece of real estate that the government no doubt considered a generous gift, having previously owned all of it. They were friendly with neighboring mountain tribes but suffered frequently from the incursions of their bitter enemies the Blackfeet, who came across the Continental Divide from the plains on horse stealing and scalp raising expeditions. It was a lively neighborhood, and one had to keep a sharp eye out for visitors.

First white men in here were trappers and traders for British fur companies as early as 1809. They were a hardy breed, these men who sought their fortunes in beaver pelts, and they left their names on the land, if not much else. Placer discoveries were made and mining operations commenced about sixty years later, bringing a new kind of man to the river, one who sought his fortune in gold dust rather than fur.

The river itself, a cold, clear stream that runs through a deep canyon, is a highway for waterfowl and a home for fish. It is a place of quiet beauty, where a man might sit on the bank and watch the water flow by, and think about the people who have come and gone, and the people who are yet to come. It is a river that has seen much, and will see much more, before it finally reaches the sea.
