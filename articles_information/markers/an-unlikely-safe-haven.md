# An Unlikely Safe Haven

**By Mark Twain**

*[East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 1910]*

When the world is on fire, a hole in the ground starts to look like a luxury hotel. On the evening of August 20, 1910, the Bitterroot Mountains decided to burn themselves down, and they did not care who was standing in the way. The sky turned yellow, the wind roared like a freight train, and the heat was so intense it could blister the paint on a boxcar from a mile away.

The people who lived along the Milwaukee Road had three choices: jump in a river and hope it did not boil, run for their lives and hope they were faster than a gale-force wind, or get on a train and hope the engineer knew what he was doing. Engineer C. H. Marshall was one of the men who tried to do the impossible. He stopped his train for everyone he saw stranded along the tracks, loading them into cars that were already smoking from the heat.

"Fires of yesterday and last night have swept practically all the country from Avery to St. Regis," reported E. J. Pearson, the Chief Engineer of the Puget Sound Railroad. "Nothing could have lived in the mountains last evening except for the tunnels."

Marshall realized he could not outrun the fire, so he did the only sensible thing left to do: he drove his train straight into a mountain. He sped toward a tunnel, the roar of the fire so deafening that no one on board could stand upright, and parked his smoking train in the dark. It was a terrifying place to be, but it was considerably better than being outside.

Another train found refuge in the nearly two-mile-long Taft Tunnel. The men, women, and children huddled in the damp, cold darkness, listening to the inferno rage outside, waiting for the end of the world to pass them by. They survived because the railroad had spent millions of dollars blasting holes through solid rock, never imagining that the most valuable thing those holes would ever hold was a train full of terrified people.
