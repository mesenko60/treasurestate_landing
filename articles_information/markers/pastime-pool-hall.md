# Pastime Pool Hall

**By Editor**

*Whitefish, Flathead County, Montana, April 2026*

This venerable building has a long and colorful history as a Whitefish watering place and entertainment center, which is a polite way of saying it has been a place where men went to lose their money and their sobriety since 1903. Called the Dodge House when it was built, it eventually became known as the Pastime bar, a name it retained for many years, until it was renamed the Bulldog Saloon in the early 1980s, in honor of the Whitefish Bulldog high school athletic teams. As of 2002, it remained the Bulldog, a flamboyantly decorated sports bar, a place where the primary sport was likely the consumption of alcohol and the telling of lies.

In the teen years of the twentieth century, the building was called Houston's Hall, after Dr. Houston, a railroad physician and surgeon who had offices on the second floor, a convenient arrangement for a town that specialized in railroad accidents and bar fights. The medical offices later were occupied by other doctors, men who presumably found the location equally advantageous. Occasionally used for boxing matches over the years, the second floor also served as an early meeting place for Masons and the Order of the Eastern Star, a combination of activities that suggests a certain flexibility in the moral architecture of the town. The space later fell into disrepair and was used for storage of city Christmas decorations and other items, a sad end for a room that had seen so much blood and brotherhood.

Meanwhile the main business survived Prohibition by selling soft drinks, cigars and fruit and offering billiards and pool tables for the recreation of locals, many of them railroaders, a story that is as common as it is unlikely. In 1933, the Whitefish Pilot reported that a beer license had been issued to J.L. Akey, the new owner of the Pastime, a man who was finally able to sell legally what he had likely been selling illegally for years. The Akey family was associated with the Pastime for some fifty years, a remarkable run in a business that usually ruins its proprietors in half that time. Earl and Jacqueline (Akey) Schommer were the last of the family to run the establishment, and after another ownership change, it was purchased by Linda and Buck May, who inherited a building that had seen more history than most towns.
