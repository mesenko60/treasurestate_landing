# The Mighty Missouri: Lifeblood of the Plains

**By editor**

*Roy, Fergus County, Montana*

The Missouri River courses through the plains like a great serpent of water sculpting a verdant vein across an otherwise arid expanse. Here, the river’s presence contrasts sharply with the vast, wind-battered prairie that stretches beyond its banks--an expanse dominated by sagebrush (Artemisia tridentata), blue grama (Bouteloua gracilis), and intermittent stands of ponderosa pine (Pinus ponderosa) rising on distant ridges. This river, born in the high Rocky Mountains of Montana, traces a sinuous path for over 2,300 miles before joining the Mississippi, yet it is here in central Montana’s Fergus County that the Missouri reveals its character as both provider and thoroughfare.

For millennia, the river nourished the peoples who found sustenance along its shores. Archaeological sites near Roy and upstream reveal ancient camps and bison kill sites, where indigenous hunters followed the migrations of the American bison (Bison bison) across the plains. These sites, some dating back over 8,000 years, attest to a profound human reliance upon the Missouri’s water and the ecosystems it supported. The river’s floodplains nurtured wild turnip (Pediomelum esculentum) and chokecherry (Prunus virginiana), staples in the diets of the Blackfeet, Gros Ventre, and other tribes whose lives intertwined with the river’s rhythms.

The Missouri’s role as a corridor of exploration and expansion became unmistakable with the arrival of the Lewis and Clark Expedition. On May 24, 1805, the Corps of Discovery reached this very stretch of the river during their second year of traversing the West. Meriwether Lewis recorded in his journal, “The river is very crooked, the current swift and the shores lined with cottonwood (Populus deltoides). We made slow progress, pulling and poling upstream, covering about thirteen miles this day.” The party camped just two and a half miles upriver from what is now Roy, Montana, marking the beginning of their passage through the vast Missouri Breaks--rugged badlands carved by the river’s relentless erosive power over the last ten thousand years since the last glacial retreat.

The Missouri here incises through sedimentary rock formations of the Hell Creek Formation and the Fort Union Formation, layers deposited during the Paleocene epoch some 66 million years ago. These strata hold the fossilized remains of ancient life forms, including early mammals and the last of the non-avian dinosaurs. The river’s continually shifting channel exposes these layers, providing a living geological record of deep time.

Following closely upon the expedition’s footsteps came fur traders who, like the native peoples before them, recognized the river’s value as a transportation highway and resource hub. Among these figures, James Kipp is particularly notable. Born in Montreal in 1788, Kipp embodied the rugged determination required to navigate both the Missouri’s treacherous currents and the complex relations with indigenous tribes. In 1831, he established Fort Piegan at the confluence of the Marias and Missouri rivers, 128 river miles above Roy. This outpost was the American Fur Company’s first trading post west of Fort Union, itself situated near the Montana-North Dakota border. Kipp’s efforts opened avenues for commerce and cultural exchange, and his command of steamboat navigation on the Missouri was unmatched.

The Missouri’s waters, however, are no gentle highway. The river’s swift currents, shifting sandbars, and seasonal floods made navigation perilous. Early fur traders struggled with keelboats, laboriously poling and pulling upriver against relentless flow. The Corps of Discovery averaged a mere thirteen miles a day. As Kipp later observed, “The Missouri is a mighty force that demands respect; to master it requires patience, skill, and a will as strong as the river’s current.” His words speak to the river’s dual nature as both barrier and conduit.

The arrival of steamboats in the latter half of the 19th century transformed the Missouri into a thoroughfare of commerce and migration. In 1860, the steamboat Chippewa reached Fort Benton, the furthest navigable point upriver. Fort Benton, near the mouth of the Marias River, became the last port of call before the rugged mountains and plains beyond. Over the ensuing three decades, dozens of steamboats plied the Missouri, ferrying miners, settlers, and supplies into Montana’s interior. The boats returned laden with gold from the burgeoning mining districts, pelts harvested from abundant wildlife, buffalo robes collected during the great bison hunts, and eventually, cattle and wool from the expanding ranches.

This river commerce shaped the economic and social landscape of Montana. Towns like Fort Benton thrived as supply hubs, while the Missouri’s waters sustained agriculture and livestock. Yet, the river also posed challenges for crossing. Until the late 1950s, the only means to traverse the river between Fort Peck Dam and Fort Benton were ferries and fords. The construction of the Fred Robinson Bridge in 1959 marked a significant engineering achievement, connecting communities and easing transit across the river’s breadth.

Today, this stretch of the Upper Missouri enjoys protection as a National Wild and Scenic River, preserving its natural character and scenic grandeur. The river’s course remains a corridor for migrating birds, including the American white pelican (Pelecanus erythrorhynchos) and the great blue heron (Ardea herodias), and its waters teem with native fish such as the paddlefish (Polyodon spathula) and the Missouri River trout (Salvelinus fontinalis). The cottonwood galleries lining the banks provide nesting habitat for bald eagles (Haliaeetus leucocephalus), whose silhouettes grace the sky.

Standing on the banks near Roy, one can witness the interplay of geological time and human history carved by the Missouri’s ceaseless flow. The river’s waters, shaped by snowmelt from the Lewis Range and the Highwood Mountains, carry with them sediments that have sculpted the surrounding badlands and nourished the plains. This enduring artery has nourished life and carried ambitions, from indigenous hunters to explorers, traders, and settlers.

As William Clark once noted during the expedition, “The Missouri is the great artery of this vast continent, whose waters invite discovery and promise sustenance.” These words capture the river’s significance far beyond its waters -- as a force shaping landscapes, ecosystems, and human destinies alike.
