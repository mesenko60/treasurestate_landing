# Name That River

**By Editor**

*Missoula, Missoula County, Montana*

Naming a river ought to be a straightforward piece of business. You find a river, you give it a name, and you move on. But when Meriwether Lewis and William Clark arrived in the Missoula Valley in September 1805, they seemed to suffer from a sudden crisis of indecision.

They were camped about ten miles south of here, at Traveler's Rest, when Lewis sent two men down to investigate the confluence of what we now call the Bitterroot and Clark Fork rivers. On September 10, Lewis wrote in his journal: "...this fork of the river we determined to name the Valley plain river."

It was a perfectly good name, descriptive and accurate, but it did not last the week. 

A few days later, they decided to name the Bitterroot River the "Flathead River," operating under the mistaken impression that the local Salish Indians were called Flatheads. Then, having successfully misnamed one river after a tribe that did not exist by that name, they reconsidered the whole arrangement and renamed the whole system "Clark's River."

This set a dangerous precedent. Once a river has been named three times in a week, everybody else feels entitled to take a turn. 

Over the next century, the "Valley plain river" that flows through downtown Missoula was subjected to a relentless barrage of nomenclature. It was called the Arrow Stone River. It was called the Hell Gate River. It was called the Missoula River. 

The name "Missoula" itself was the result of a similar process of linguistic erosion. In 1812, the British fur trader and mapmaker David Thompson came through the valley and recorded the local Indian word for the area: *Nemissoolatako*. The Americans, who have never met a five-syllable word they couldn't improve by chopping off the ends, eventually whittled it down to "Missoula."

Today, the river that runs through town is officially the Clark Fork of the Columbia River. It travels over 1,300 miles from the Continental Divide to the Pacific Ocean, carrying the name of a man who only saw a fraction of it. 

It is a fine name, and it seems to have stuck, but if you stand on the bank long enough, you get the feeling the river doesn't much care what you call it, so long as you stay out of its way when the spring runoff comes.
