# "Removal" of Salish from Bitterroot Valley, 1855-1891

**By Manus AI**

*Stevensville, Ravalli County, Montana, October 1891*

In the Hellgate Treaty negotiations of 1855, Chief Victor, known to his people as Xwetxxcln (Plenty Horses), rebuffed the efforts of United States officials to persuade the Selíš to abandon the choice lands of their ancestral Bitterroot Valley. He stood firm, and the government, having other matters to attend to, let the issue rest for a time. But the settlers who followed the gold strikes and the railroads were not so patient, and after Victor died, they successfully lobbied President Ulysses S. Grant to declare the Flathead Reservation "better adapted to the wants of the Flathead tribe" in 1872.

Congress, in its infinite wisdom, sent future President James A. Garfield to arrange for the removal of the Selíš. Chief Chariot, or Stmxé Owoxqeys (Claw of Small Grizzly), informed Garfield that the Bitterroot was where the bones of his ancestors were buried, and he would not leave. Garfield, possessing the political acumen that would later elevate him to the highest office in the land, simply forged Chariot's "x" mark onto the agreement and declared the matter settled. More white settlers moved illegally onto Selíš lands, and the pressure intensified with the construction of the Missoula & Bitter Root Valley Railroad in 1888.

In November 1889, faced with worsening conditions for his people, Chief Chariot finally agreed to leave. The Selíš therefore planted no crops the following spring, expecting to be relocated. Congress, however, delayed funding for the removal for two years, pushing many of the people to the brink of starvation. It was a masterstroke of bureaucratic efficiency, ensuring that the Selíš would be too weak to resist when the time finally came.

Finally, in October 1891, General Henry Carrington and troops from Fort Missoula roughly pushed the tribe on the sad march north to the Reservation. The government reneged on the promised aid for relocation, but the Selíš nevertheless managed to rebuild their lives in the Jocko Valley, proving that while the United States government could take their land, it could not entirely extinguish their spirit.
