# Cadillac Hotel

**By Mark Twain**

*Whitefish, Flathead County, Montana, 1907*

For most of the twentieth century, the corner of Central Avenue and Railway Street was the site of the Cadillac Hotel. It was not the first hotel in town; that distinction went to the Dodge Hotel, which had the good sense to be built before anyone else thought of it. But the Cadillac, a three-story brick affair erected in 1907, had the advantage of proximity to the Great Northern Railway depot. It became a popular hostelry, catering not only to railroaders and passengers but to the denizens of Central Avenue's bars and gambling places, who found it convenient to have a bed within staggering distance of their amusements.

In 1922, J.J. Cremans built the New Cadillac Hotel, which was to stand for the next seventy-two years. It passed through the hands of various owners, including John Bender, Dick and Martha Zerr, and later their son Jack, three skiers, Nancy Montgomery, Bob Cavill and Jack Tidyman, and Mel and Evelyn Stenslie. The Cadillac Bar, which adjoined the hotel rooms on the south, was a town gathering place. For a short time at mid-century, the organist there was Joan Smith, who later became Mrs. Ray Kroc, wife of the McDonald's magnate and a well-known philanthropist. One imagines she played with a certain brisk efficiency, perhaps anticipating a future where billions would be served.

The bar and restaurant was rechristened the Hanging Tree in the 1960s, after the book of the same name by author Dorothy Johnson, who was a resident of Whitefish. It is a curious choice of name for an establishment seeking to attract patrons, suggesting as it does a rather permanent end to the evening's festivities, but perhaps it appealed to the local sense of humor.

The hotel, suffering the effects of old age and disuse, eventually closed. In 1994, the old brick building was demolished to make way for the construction of a modern brewery, the Great Northern Brewing Co. The bar and restaurant later became the Glacier Grande, then Serano's, and then Paddle & Axe. In 2006, under the new ownership of Bill Foley, it reopened as Craggy Range Bar & Grill. The building is gone, but the thirst remains, a constant in the ever-changing landscape of the American West.
