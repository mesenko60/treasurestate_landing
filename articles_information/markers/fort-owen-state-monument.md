# Fort Owen State Monument

**By Manus AI**

*Stevensville, Ravalli County, Montana, 1850*

In the fall of 1850, a thirty-two-year-old former army sutler from Pennsylvania named John Owen arrived in the valley of the Bitterroot River with his common-law Shoshone wife, Nancy. They made the harrowing journey by ox-cart, having heard of the varied attractions of the Bitterroot Valley, which the men of Isaac Stevens's western railroad survey were soon to characterize as "the valley of perennial spring." Owen paid $250, cash, for the physical remains of St. Mary's Mission, a bargain that would have made any real estate speculator proud.

The Jesuit fathers who had established the mission nearby in 1841 had closed their doors in 1850, leaving behind a collection of log and adobe walls that had witnessed dramatic changes as the valley emerged from remote wilderness to settled agricultural community. Owen immediately began developing his property into what was to become, for a short time, one of the most important commercial centers in the Northwest. He operated and expanded the mills, cultivated the fields, enlarged the fort, and kept a well-stocked trade room, thereby transforming the mission compound into a vibrant trading post complex.

A man of many talents, Owen also served as agent to the Flathead Nation from 1856 to 1862, and for a time the fort was agency headquarters. He and Nancy created a refined and comfortable haven in the vast timberland, extending gracious hospitality to Indians, traders, trappers, missionaries, settlers, and travelers. With the 1860s came gold-seekers and a fresh clientele, but the newly completed Mullan Road bypassed Fort Owen, and trading dwindled. It is a common enough story in the West: a man builds an empire, and the government builds a road that goes somewhere else.

Upon Nancy's death in 1868, Owen's mental health deteriorated, and in 1872, Fort Owen was sold at sheriff's sale to Washington J. McCormick, who operated the fort's mills until 1889. In 1937, the site was donated to the State of Montana, and the donors sponsored stabilization and partial reconstruction of its sole remaining building, the East Barracks. Since 1971, the Stevensville Historical Society has been instrumental in interpretive reconstruction, continued stabilization, and maintenance of this noteworthy site, ensuring that at least one piece of John Owen's empire remains standing.
