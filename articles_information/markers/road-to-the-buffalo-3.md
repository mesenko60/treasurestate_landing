# Road to the Buffalo

**By Editor**

*Lincoln, Lewis and Clark County, Montana, August 2022*

The great shaggy beast sustained the Indians of the Plains and adjacent areas for thousands of years, and the paths they walked to find them were older than the memory of the men who later claimed to discover them. In these parts we call Montana, an ancient network of trails led the Salish, Pend d'Oreille, and related Indian tribes, whose territory once straddled both sides of the Continental Divide, into prime bison country on the high plains of central and eastern Montana. The Nez Perce called this ancient trail leading through traditional Salish-Pend d'Oreille territory "Qoq'aalx 'Iskit," meaning "Buffalo Road." It was a well-worn path that crossed the Continental Divide at a low pass the Salish called "Smitu Sxcucs.i," which means Indian Fort Pass, a place where warriors sat in small stone structures to watch for approaching Blackfeet raiders. Today, the Americans know this landmark as Lewis and Clark Pass, named for men who walked it only once.

When Meriwether Lewis and his party crossed this divide on July 3, 1806, they did not find their own way; they followed the road the Nez Perce showed them, a well-beaten track that led up the east branch of Clark's river and a river called Cokahlarishkit, or the river of the road to buffalo, and thence to the falls of the Missouri. The invasion of the West by EuroAmericans led to the virtual extermination of the bison by 1883, with devastating impacts for many tribes who had depended on the herds for food, clothing, shelter, tools, and spiritual sustenance. Despite these losses, Indian people survived, and today they still maintain an intimate relationship with these important cultural landscapes, remembering the days when they used dogs and, in later years, horses to transport their goods and supplies with a travois, whose tracks can still be seen along the trail.
