# Duncan Samson Block

**By Editor**

*Whitefish, Flathead County, Montana, April 2026*

This staid old brick building has a rich and colorful history, which is a polite way of saying it has survived a century of Whitefish weather and a variety of tenants. The Duncan Samson Block, built in 1910 at a cost of about $32,000, was the third or fourth brick building in the fledgling town of Whitefish, a place that was just beginning to realize it might be permanent. Mrs. Jemima Duncan, a widow who had moved to Whitefish from Kalispell a few years earlier, saw opportunity in constructing a rooming house for employees of the Great Northern Railway, which had just named Whitefish as a division point on its line. It was a sound business plan, based on the reliable premise that railroad men need a place to sleep and are generally willing to pay for it.

Along the way, she met J.A. Samson, a tie contractor for the railroad, and they were married the same year the building was completed, setting up housekeeping in one of the downstairs apartments. Many of the building's tenants were single young men, and as they married, Mrs. Samson advanced into the real estate business, finding small homes to sell them, operating her office out of her apartment. It was a neat piece of vertical integration: she housed them when they were single, sold them a house when they married, and presumably collected a commission on both ends.

Although the building always has been primarily an apartment house, over the years it also housed a shoe store, a couple of grocery stores, a succession of chiropractor's offices starting in the 1930s, and a tax preparation business, proving that the only things certain in life are death, taxes, and back pain. In 1982, local contractor Gary Tallman bought the building and carried out a major modernization of the housing units, selling them as condominiums. In the 21st Century the Block, still essentially an apartment house, belongs to the owners of eight second-floor apartments and several main-floor businesses, including a coffee shop, a landscape architect and yes, a chiropractor office. Its exterior differs very little from the sturdy brick structure that rose along Second Street more than a century ago, a testament to the enduring value of a good brick wall.
