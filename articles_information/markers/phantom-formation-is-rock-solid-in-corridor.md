# Phantom Formation Is Rock Solid In Corridor

**By Editor**

*Paradise, Sanders County, Montana, July 2011*

The Prichard Formation is a layer of rock so old and so deep that nobody has ever found the bottom of it, which is a comforting thought in a world where everything else seems to be falling apart. It dates back 1.5 billion years, making it one of the oldest exposed sedimentary rocks in western Montana. It is over four miles thick at the confluence of the Clark Fork and Flathead rivers, a fact that should give pause to anyone who thinks they can dig a hole to China.

The rock was formed when layer upon layer of sand, silt, and clay was deposited from a river delta into a sea that covered parts of what is now Montana, Idaho, Washington, and Canada. Over millions of years, heat and pressure turned the sediments into a hard, metamorphic rock called argillite. The uplifting and shifting of the mountains then tilted the Prichard on its side, exposing ripple marks and mud cracks that are evidence of its sedimentary origin.

Because the mudstone layers in the Prichard split apart to form flagstone, it has been quarried to provide colorful construction material for hearths, stone paths, and facings for buildings. In addition, a slate-like rock found in the formation was used by early people to make tools and weapons. It is a rock of many uses, provided you have the patience to chip away at it for a few thousand years. The marker stands as a testament to the enduring power of geology, and a reminder that the earth will still be here long after we have finished making a mess of it.
