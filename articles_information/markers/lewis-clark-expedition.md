# Lewis & Clark Expedition

**By editor**

*Cardwell, Jefferson County, Montana, August 1805*

On August 1, 1805, the Lewis and Clark Expedition camped at a point 200 yards west of the marker's location, on the south bank of the Jefferson River, at the mouth of the creek that flows in from the north. Meriwether Lewis and three companions had crossed the mountains to the northeast and come down the North Boulder Valley, reaching the river at two in the afternoon. They found a herd of elk grazing in the meadow and killed two of them. They ate elk steaks for lunch and left the remaining meat on the riverbank for the main party's dinner.

Captain Clark arrived late in the evening with the boats, after a strenuous day spent hauling the dugout canoes up the canyon rapids by means of a long rawhide tow line that had broken in the rapids below. The men were hungry. The sight of the two elk decided the matter. They pitched camp.

Reuben and Joseph Fields went on a short hunt up the creek and killed five deer in the willow breaks, which caused the stream to be named Field's Creek. It is now known as North Boulder Creek. A large brown bear was seen on the south side of the river. Clark shot a bighorn sheep in the canyon. Lewis shot two antelope a short distance upstream. Near camp was observed the first Clark's nutcracker known to science -- the bird that Lewis called the Maximilian Jay, which was later renamed in Clark's honor by the ornithologist Alexander Wilson.

The temperature at sunrise on August 2 was fifty degrees above zero.

This is the kind of entry that appears in the Lewis and Clark journals with a frequency that becomes, after a while, astonishing. Every day brought new animals, new plants, new geographical features. Every day the men were hungry and tired and working harder than most people work in a lifetime. Every day Lewis wrote it down.

Lewis had been instructed by President Jefferson to document everything: the geography, the climate, the plants, the animals, the Indian nations, the mineral resources. Lewis carried out these instructions with a thoroughness that bordered on obsession. He collected 178 plants new to science. He documented 122 animal species. He produced the first accurate maps of the Missouri and Columbia river systems. He wrote descriptions of the landscape that are still useful to geologists and ecologists two centuries later.

On August 1, 1805, Lewis wrote in his journal: "we saw a great number of Beaver in the course of this day, and the men with me killed several of them. the river is still rapid and the water is clear and cold. the mountains on either side are high and rugged, and the timber is principally pine and fir. the grass in the bottoms is good and the elk are numerous." He wrote this after a day of crossing mountains, killing elk, and making camp in a strange valley. He wrote it because Jefferson had told him to write everything down, and because Lewis was the kind of man who did what he was told to do, and did it thoroughly.

The Jefferson River, which the expedition was ascending on August 1, had been named by Lewis and Clark for the president who had sent them. They had named the three forks of the Missouri -- the Jefferson, the Madison, and the Gallatin -- for the president and his two cabinet secretaries. This was the kind of naming that explorers do: they carry the names of the powerful into the wilderness and attach them to the landscape, so that the powerful can feel that they own something they have never seen.

The expedition reached the Three Forks of the Missouri on July 27, 1805, five days before the camp at this marker. They recognized the confluence from Sacagawea's description of the country where she had been born. She had been taken from this country by Hidatsa raiders when she was a child, perhaps ten or eleven years old. She had not seen it in five or six years. She recognized it anyway.

The Corps of Discovery crossed the Continental Divide at Lemhi Pass on August 12, 1805, eleven days after the camp at this marker. They descended the western slope into present-day Idaho, found the Shoshone people, obtained horses, and began the long crossing of the Bitterroot Mountains that nearly killed them. They reached the Pacific Ocean in November 1805 and returned to St. Louis in September 1806. The journey had taken two years, four months, and ten days.

The Clark's nutcracker that Lewis observed near this camp on August 1, 1805, is a gray and black bird that lives in the high mountains of the West. It caches pine seeds in the fall and retrieves them in the winter and spring. It has a memory for the locations of thousands of individual caches. It is still common in the mountains above the Jefferson River valley.
