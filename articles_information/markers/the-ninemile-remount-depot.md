# The Ninemile Remount Depot

**By Editor**

*Huson, Missoula County, Montana, April 2026*

The Ninemile Remount was, at one time, the absolute center for the U.S. Forest Service packing activities in the Northern Rockies, a place where the business of moving supplies into the wilderness was taken as seriously as the movement of an army. Completed by the Civilian Conservation Corps in 1935, the Remount served as the home roost for more than fifteen hundred Rocky Mountain Canaries—also known as mules—as well as a prime breeding stock for the Forest Service. Firefighting, trail and fire lookout building, and many other kinds of backcountry work were done with Ninemile packstock wearing US brands on their hips and diamond hitches cinching down their cargo. The pack strings were spurred on by packers who had reputations for never sparing the adjectives when the going got tough, men who understood that a mule responds best to a combination of precise profanity and professional respect.

The Remount's upper hayfield was used to train smokejumpers in the early years of the famous firefighting crews, a dangerous business that required men to drop from the sky into burning timber. More often than not, droning Ford TriMotor airplanes had to buzz the field to clear it of grazing livestock before the jumpers could hit the silk, an arrangement that must have surprised the mules as much as the men. It was a practical solution to a practical problem, the sort of thing that happens when you are trying to invent a new way to fight fires while simultaneously running a massive livestock operation.

In 1980, Ninemile was listed on the National Register of Historic Places for its traditional architecture and its role in Forest Service, Civilian Conservation Corps, and local history. Ninemile is still a working ranger station and pack depot, the home of a number of USFS activities, combining history and practicality in a way that is increasingly rare. Mules and horses are still run through the chutes and corrals on their way to perform important work in the backcountry, carrying on a tradition that began nearly a century ago. And if you listen very carefully, you can still hear the canaries sing, a sound that means the work is being done.
