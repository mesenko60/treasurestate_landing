# When the Mountains Roared

**By Mark Twain**

*[East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 1910]*

The 1910 fire season started like any other, which is to say it started with a few smoldering campfires, some sparks from a passing locomotive, and a handful of lightning strikes that no one paid much attention to. It was a dry summer, but summers in the West are always dry, and the people who lived there were used to a little smoke in the air.

Then came the wind. In late August, a cold front pushed gale-force winds through the region, and the little fires decided to join forces and become one massive, terrifying inferno. The sky turned a sickly yellow, ash fell like snow, and the noise was something you could feel in your bones.

"The fire by this time was an awe-inspiring spectacle," reported Roy A. Phillips, a Lolo Forest Guard who had a front-row seat to the end of the world. "The whole horizon to the west was aflame and the noise caused by the falling timber was terrific."

Down in the valley, the two hundred residents of Taft, a town that existed primarily to separate railroad workers from their paychecks, were not particularly concerned. When the young forest rangers came down from the hills to beg for help fighting the fire, the good citizens of Taft rolled whiskey barrels into the street and told the rangers to go away. They were having a party, and they did not want to be interrupted by a little thing like a wall of flame.

But the fire did not care about their party. When the flames finally crested the ridge and started down the mountain, the residents of Taft suddenly realized that whiskey was not going to save them. They abandoned their barrels, scrambled aboard the trains heading for Missoula, and left their town to burn. It was a spectacular exit, and the only thing they left behind was a lesson in humility that the mountains delivered free of charge.
