# Douse the Flames and Climb Aboard

**By Mark Twain**

*[East Portal, Mineral County, Montana, August 1910]*

When the world is on fire, a train is a very good thing to have, provided the tracks are not melting and the bridges are not burning. In August 1910, the Milwaukee Road was the only way out of the Bitterroot Mountains, and the men who drove the engines were the only hope for the people who lived along the line.

Engineer John Mackedon and his fireman were riding west toward Avery, Idaho, surrounded by a fire that was so hot it consumed pick handles lying in the open beside the track. They were worried they would not make it out alive, which was a reasonable concern given the circumstances. "The whole twenty-five miles of railroad…between Avery and the Taft Tunnel was swept by a consuming blast of fire," reported Forest Supervisor Elers Koch.

Then the emergency call came in. More fires had sent men, women, and children fleeing from their homesteads and logging camps to huddle on the railroad platform in Falcon, sixty-four miles northeast of Avery. They were begging to be rescued, and Mackedon was the only one who could do it.

He backed his train into Falcon and discovered that the buildings and railcars were already on fire. The crowd rushed toward the engine in a panic, which is what people do when they are about to burn to death. Mackedon spotted a boxcar that was not completely ablaze, told the frightened crowd to douse the fire, and ordered them to quickly climb aboard.

It was a desperate gamble, but it worked. After escaping Falcon, Mackedon stopped twice more to put out fires on the bridges in his path, because a burning bridge is a very bad thing for a train to cross. He and his fireman finally saved the stranded refugees by taking them into a tunnel to wait out the fire, proving that sometimes the best way to survive a disaster is to find a hole in the ground and stay there until the smoke clears.
