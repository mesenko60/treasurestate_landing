# A wonderful Piece of Engineering: The Mullan Road

**By Editor**

*Saltese, Mineral County, Montana, May 2018*

When Lieutenant John Mullan and his two hundred and thirty workers, soldiers, and teamsters set out in July 1859 to build a six-hundred-and-twenty-four-mile wagon road from Walla Walla, Washington, to Fort Benton, Montana, they were not merely moving dirt; they were attempting to master a wilderness that had no intention of being mastered. The topography between Lake Coeur d'Alene and the Missoula Valley was a tangle of towering mountains, serpentine rivers, steep rugged hillsides, low swampy areas, deep ravines, and fallen timbers that would have broken the spirit of lesser men. Mullan's crew, working from the sixteenth of August to the fourth of December, cut through a densely timbered section of one hundred miles, building small bridges where required and grading thousands of places. They constructed forty-seven bridges across the St. Regis-DeBorgia River between base St. Regis Pass and its confluence with the Clark Fork twenty-eight miles east of here. The work was heavy, and as Mullan himself noted in 1863, justice could not be done to the industry and fortitude of the men who drove the road through the rock and the mud.

The road, although primitive, was a triumph of engineering and a tribute to Mullan's ability and his optimism about the future of the Pacific Northwest and Montana. Originally intended as a military road, it was only used in that capacity once, instead becoming an important emigrant and freight road in western and central Montana. Unfortunately, the Mullan Road west of Missoula was not heavily used by pioneers because of the difficult terrain. Indeed, within a couple of years after its completion, most of the bridges had washed out and timber and landslides had blocked portions of the road. By the 1870s, the Mullan Road through this area was little more than a pack trail. The road still exists in places through here, high on mountainsides overlooking Interstate 90, a silent monument to the men who wrenched and jolted their wagons terribly, causing their poor mules infinite misery, as Randall Hewett recorded in 1862, winding over a trail made barely passable in a country whose scenery was the wildest ever gazed upon.
