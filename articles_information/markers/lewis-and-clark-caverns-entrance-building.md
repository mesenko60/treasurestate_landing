# Lewis and Clark Caverns Entrance Building

**By editor**

*Cardwell, Jefferson County, Montana, 1950*

The Lewis and Clark Caverns are the largest known limestone cavern in the Northwest, which is a distinction that the caverns did not ask for and cannot appreciate, being underground. They were first explored by non-Indians in the 1890s, by a rancher named Dan Morrison who found the entrance on his property and went in to see what was there. What was there was approximately three miles of decorated limestone chambers, stalactites, stalagmites, helictites, and other formations that geologists have names for and that ordinary people simply call remarkable.

President Theodore Roosevelt designated the caverns a national monument in 1908, which is what Roosevelt did with things he liked: he designated them. The designation meant that the federal government now owned them, which was an improvement over private ownership in the sense that the federal government was less likely to charge admission and more likely to let the public in. The caverns were transferred to the State of Montana in 1937, becoming the state's first state park.

In 1939, park management began plans to build a greeting center and caretaker residence at the entrance to the park. This was a reasonable plan. The park had been receiving visitors since 1937, and the visitors needed somewhere to be greeted. The plans were put on hold when the park closed during the latter half of World War II, which is the kind of thing that happens to plans when there is a war: they wait.

In 1948, the State Parks Commission hired its own architect, a man named Burt Gewalt, to begin work on a park entrance building. Gewalt produced an A-frame design that was heavily influenced by the buildings of the Civilian Conservation Corps, which had spent the 1930s constructing public facilities in parks and forests across the country. The CCC aesthetic was characterized by the use of local stone, heavy timber, and a general impression of permanence that was intended to suggest that the government had been here before and intended to stay. Gewalt's building used local stone for the rock work, drinking fountain, flagstaff, sidewalks, and parking spaces. The landscaping was reminiscent of the CCC era. The whole composition said: this is a public place, and you are welcome here.

The building was completed in 1950 and became Montana's first park visitor entrance building. This is a modest distinction, but it is a real one. Before 1950, Montana's state parks did not have entrance buildings. Visitors arrived, looked around, and left, without anyone greeting them or explaining what they were looking at. The Gewalt building changed that. It was the first official point of welcome in the Montana state park system.

Gewalt's work can also be found in Glacier National Park at Lake McDonald, where he designed a similar structure in the same period. The Lake McDonald building is still standing. The Lewis and Clark Caverns entrance building served visitors for over fifty years before it was replaced. The site where it stood is marked here.

The caverns themselves were formed over millions of years by the dissolution of limestone by slightly acidic groundwater. The limestone was deposited as marine sediment in a shallow sea that covered this part of Montana approximately 350 million years ago. The sea retreated, the sediment was compressed into rock, the rock was uplifted by the geological forces that built the Rocky Mountains, and then the water went to work on it. The result is a series of chambers decorated with formations that took hundreds of thousands of years to grow at a rate of approximately one cubic inch per century.

Lewis and Clark did not, in fact, visit the caverns. They passed through this valley in August 1805, ascending the Jefferson River toward the Continental Divide, but they did not find the caverns. The caverns were named for them anyway, because in Montana, as in much of the West, Lewis and Clark's names have been attached to a great many things that Lewis and Clark never saw. This is not dishonest, exactly. It is more a matter of enthusiasm.
