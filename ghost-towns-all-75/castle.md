# Castle

**By Editor**

*Meagher County*

## History

The mountains southwest of White Sulphur Springs are the kind of country that looks ordinary from a distance and reveals its character only when you get close enough to see the rock. The Castle Mountains, as they came to be known, are a compact range of metamorphic and intrusive rock that rises abruptly from the surrounding plains of Meagher County, and it was in this rock that a prospector named Hector Horton found silver ore in 1884. What he found was good enough to stake a claim, and the claim he called the North Carolina mine was the first of nearly a thousand that would be registered in the Castle district over the next seven years.

The pace of development was rapid even by the standards of Montana's silver era. Within a year of Horton's discovery, other prospectors had spread across the Castle Mountains, sinking test shafts and following the ore-bearing veins through the rock. The geology was favorable: the silver occurred in replacement deposits and veins cutting through the older metamorphic basement, and the ore was rich enough in the best locations to justify the investment of serious capital. By 1890 the district had attracted the attention of outside investors, and the Cumberland mine, which would become the district's leading producer, was being developed on a systematic basis.

The town of Castle was incorporated in 1891, by which time it had grown to a population of approximately 1,500 people. The influx of miners had brought with it the full complement of frontier commercial life: merchants, saloon keepers, boarding house operators, and the various service providers who followed the mining economy wherever it went. At its peak, Castle had a fine school, numerous merchants, several fraternal organizations, a jail, seven brothels, and as many as fourteen saloons. The commercial district along the main street was substantial enough to include a bakery, a general store with a post office, a meat market, and a furniture store, the kind of infrastructure that suggested a community expecting to be permanent.

The most famous resident of Castle's brief heyday was Martha Jane Cannary Burke, known to history as Calamity Jane. She arrived in Castle in the early 1890s and attempted to establish a restaurant with her companion, M. Burk. The enterprise did not prosper. The local newspapers reported that "Mr. and Mrs. M. Burk, who started a restaurant here a few weeks ago, were arrested last Sunday morning" on charges of absconding debtors, the charges apparently related to a team of horses the couple had acquired without paying for it. The Burks moved on quickly, eventually making their way back to Deadwood in the Black Hills, and Calamity Jane's connection to Castle was reduced to a footnote in the town's history. But it is the kind of footnote that tells you something real about the character of the place: Castle was the kind of town that attracted the restless and the ambitious, people who were always looking for the next opportunity and not always particular about how they found it.

The silver panic of 1893 struck Castle with the same force it struck every silver camp in Montana. The repeal of the Sherman Silver Purchase Act in November of that year sent silver prices into a collapse from which they did not recover for a generation, and the mines that had been the foundation of Castle's economy became instantly uneconomical. The population that had built the town in three years abandoned it in less than one. Businesses closed, buildings were boarded up, and the streets that had been crowded with miners and merchants fell quiet.

The Cumberland mine, the district's most productive operation, proved more durable than the town that surrounded it. The mine continued to operate intermittently through the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, sustained by periods of higher metal prices and by the discovery of gold values in ore that had originally been worked for silver. The mine's final closure came in 1950, and with it the last reason for anyone to maintain a presence at Castle. The few residents who had stayed through the mine's later years departed, and the town entered the long quiet of its abandonment.

The Castle Mountains Mining District produced substantial quantities of silver, gold, lead, and zinc over its working life, though the exact totals are difficult to establish from surviving records. The district's production was concentrated in the Cumberland mine and a handful of other significant producers, with the bulk of the silver coming from the years before 1893 and the gold production extending through the twentieth century's intermittent revivals.

The town site today is privately owned, and the landowners have maintained a policy of allowing visitors to view the surviving structures from the road and adjacent areas while requiring permission for access to the buildings themselves. This arrangement has preserved Castle in a state of managed decay that is unusual among Montana ghost towns, where most sites are either fully accessible public lands or completely inaccessible private property.

## What Remains Today

Castle retains a remarkable collection of surviving structures for a town of its age. The open rock basement of the baker's general store and post office is visible from the road, and across what was once the main street, the sites of Berg's meat market and Kidd's furniture store can be identified. On the far hillside, the location of Minnie's sporting house is marked by the remains of its foundation. Several other buildings survive in varying states of preservation, their heavy timber frames still standing after more than a century of abandonment.

The private ownership of the site has been a mixed blessing for preservation. The landowners have been conscientious stewards, and the absence of casual vandalism and artifact removal that plagues many accessible ghost towns has allowed Castle's surviving structures to deteriorate at the natural rate of weathering rather than the accelerated rate of human interference. The buildings that remain are genuine artifacts of the 1890s mining era, not reconstructions or stabilized ruins.

## Visiting

Castle is located just southwest of White Sulphur Springs, the Meagher County seat, via county roads heading into the Castle Mountains. The site is on private land, and visitors are expected to respect the property boundaries and obtain permission before entering the building area. Viewing from the road is generally possible without special arrangements.

White Sulphur Springs offers a full range of visitor services and is worth a stop for its own history. The Meagher County Historical Museum in White Sulphur Springs maintains exhibits on the Castle district and the broader history of the county. The drive from White Sulphur Springs to Castle passes through open ranch country with views of the Castle Mountains that give the district its name.
