# Cable

**By Editor**

*Granite County, Montana*

## History

The story of Cable begins with a piece of bad luck that turned into good fortune, which is the kind of reversal that the mining West specialized in. In 1867, three prospectors made camp in the mountains along what would become the Pintler Scenic Route in Granite County. When they woke the following morning, their horses were gone. As they searched the surrounding terrain for the animals, they found something better: a large quartz pillar loaded with gold. The horses, presumably, were never recovered. The mine was named the Atlantic Cable Lode, in tribute to the laying of the Trans-Atlantic telegraph cable, which had been completed the previous year and was the great technological achievement of the age. The camp that grew up around the mine took the shorter name: Cable.

The Atlantic Cable Lode was developed with the help of Helena financiers, who provided the capital that the three original prospectors lacked. The mine produced gold from the quartz veins of the Flint Creek Range, and for a time it was a going concern. The ore body ran through granite and quartzite, the kind of hard-rock mining that required blasting, timbering, and the constant work of keeping a shaft open against the pressure of the mountain. It was not placer work, where a man could work a gravel bar with a pan and a sluice box. It was industrial work, requiring machinery, capital, and a reliable labor force.

But the three original partners quarreled, as partners in isolated mining ventures often did. The disagreements were never fully resolved, and after a decade of operations the situation became irreconcilable. Operations ceased, and the property was sold to new owners who believed, correctly as it turned out, that the ground still had something to offer.

The new owners took over in 1883 and worked the mine until 1891, when it closed again. The camp went through the intermittent cycle that characterized many small gold operations in the Flint Creek Range: periods of activity followed by periods of abandonment, each revival dependent on the price of gold, the availability of capital, and the willingness of men to work in a remote mountain valley for wages that were never quite as good as the stories had promised. The surrounding district, which included the larger operations at Philipsburg and Granite to the north, was producing silver in enormous quantities during the 1880s, and the gold operations at Cable were modest by comparison.

The discovery of the Gold Coin Lode in 1898 brought the most productive phase of Cable's history. The Gold Coin was a different ore body from the original Atlantic Cable Lode, richer in gold and more accessible to the processing technology of the late nineteenth century. It produced approximately two hundred thousand dollars in gold over the following decade, a figure modest by the standards of the great Montana mines but substantial enough to justify continued investment and to sustain a small community of miners and their families in the valley.

In 1906, the Gold Coin Mill was built on the hillside above the camp, a substantial frame structure that processed ore from the Gold Coin Lode and gave Cable its most recognizable landmark. The mill was a significant piece of infrastructure, with stamp mills for crushing ore and amalgamation equipment for recovering gold, and its construction represented a commitment to the long-term development of the site. The mill and mine operated sporadically through the early decades of the twentieth century, sustained by the intermittent economics of small-scale gold mining and by the occasional discovery of new ore that justified continued work.

The Pintler Scenic Route, which runs through the valley, was one of the more traveled roads in Granite County, connecting the mining districts of the Flint Creek Range to the markets and supply centers of the Clark Fork Valley. Cable's position along this route gave it a modest commercial function beyond its mining operations, and the camp served as a stopping point for travelers moving between Drummond and Anaconda.

By 1940, the operation had finally run its course. The Gold Coin Mine's ore bodies were exhausted, the price of gold did not justify continued development of lower-grade material, and the labor required to keep the mine and mill operating could not be justified by the returns. The mill closed, the remaining residents departed, and Cable became a ghost town. The mill and several area buildings, which were on private property, were subsequently torn down, leaving the valley without the physical evidence of what had once been there.

The name of the mine, and of the town, is a reminder of how thoroughly the world beyond the mountains penetrated even the most remote corners of the frontier. The men who named the Atlantic Cable Lode in 1867 were aware of the technological marvels of their age and chose to honor one of them in the naming of their discovery. The Trans-Atlantic cable had been a project of enormous ambition, requiring the coordination of two governments, multiple private companies, and the work of thousands of people across two continents. That three prospectors in the mountains of Montana Territory would reach for that name when they found their gold vein says something about the aspirations of the men who worked these mountains, and about the connections between the remote West and the wider world that the West was always, despite appearances, part of.

## What Remains Today

The Gold Coin Mill, which was the most substantial structure at Cable, has been torn down. Several area buildings that once stood on private land have also been removed. What remains is largely the landscape itself: the mountain setting along the Pintler Scenic Route, the traces of the mining operations in the terrain, and the name on the map. The valley retains the character of a place where people once worked and lived, even if the physical evidence of that work has largely disappeared.

## Visiting

Cable is accessible from Montana Highway 1, the Pintler Scenic Route, between Drummond and Anaconda in Granite County, north of Silver Lake. The site is on private land, and formal visitor access is limited. The highway itself passes through the valley and provides views of the surrounding terrain. The nearest services are in Philipsburg, approximately fifteen miles to the north, a well-preserved Victorian mining town that offers a useful context for understanding the broader history of the Flint Creek Range mining district.
