# Yogo

**By Editor**

*Fergus County*

## History

Jake Hoover came to Yogo Gulch in 1895 looking for gold, which was the sensible thing to look for in the Little Belt Mountains of central Montana. He found some, enough to justify continued prospecting, and he began working the gravels of Yogo Creek in the methodical way of a man who has learned that patience pays better than excitement. What he also found, mixed in with the stream gravels, were small blue stones that he did not immediately recognize as anything valuable. They were heavier than quartz, harder than most minerals he encountered, and a peculiar shade of blue that he had not seen before in a Montana stream. He set them aside.

The stones turned out to be sapphires, and not ordinary sapphires. The Yogo sapphires that Hoover had stumbled upon in the creek gravels of a central Montana gulch were, as gemologists would eventually determine, among the finest sapphires in the world. Their color was a pure cornflower blue, neither too dark nor too pale, achieved without the heat treatment that most sapphires require to reach their best color. The Yogo stones were gem-quality in their natural state, a characteristic so unusual that it set them apart from virtually every other sapphire deposit on earth.

The source of the sapphires was a dike of igneous rock cutting through the older limestone of the Little Belt Mountains, a narrow intrusion of lamproite that had carried the sapphires up from deep in the earth when it forced its way through the overlying rock millions of years ago. Erosion had gradually broken down the dike and washed the sapphires downstream into the creek gravels where Hoover found them. The dike itself, the primary source of the gems, was identified and staked in the years following Hoover's initial discovery, and it became the focus of the organized mining operations that followed.

The New Mine Sapphire Syndicate was incorporated under Montana law in 1898, bringing the capital and the organizational structure needed to work the dike systematically. The syndicate's operations were industrial in character: the dike rock was excavated, crushed, and processed to recover the sapphires it contained, a labor-intensive process that required a permanent workforce and the infrastructure to support it. By the early twentieth century, the combined Yogo Mine had produced more than thirteen million carats of rough sapphires, a quantity that established the Yogo Gulch deposit as one of the most productive gem sapphire sources in the world.

The sapphires found uses beyond jewelry. During the First World War, the optical quality of Yogo sapphires made them valuable for military rangefinders and other precision instruments, a use that gave the mine strategic importance and helped sustain its operations during a period when luxury gem markets were disrupted by the conflict. The combination of gem and industrial demand kept the Yogo operations going through the early decades of the twentieth century, though the economics of sapphire mining were always more complicated than those of gold or silver, dependent on the vagaries of gem markets rather than the more predictable fluctuations of commodity prices.

The camp that grew up around the Yogo Mine was never a large town. It was a working camp, housing the men who operated the mine and the families who depended on them, but it lacked the commercial infrastructure of a true mining town. There was no main street lined with saloons and merchants, no fraternal hall or opera house. Yogo was a company operation in a remote gulch, and its social life was shaped by that character. The nearest real town was Lewistown, approximately sixteen miles away, and the miners and their families made the trip to town for the things that the camp could not provide.

The mine changed hands several times over the decades, passing through a succession of operators who worked it with varying degrees of success. Intergem, a modern mining company, operated the Yogo deposit in the latter part of the twentieth century, and when Intergem ceased operations, Vortex Mining began working the hills west of the Yogo Dike in 1985. The Yogo deposit has never been exhausted, and the question of how much gem-quality sapphire remains in the dike is one that has attracted renewed interest in recent years as the value of untreated natural sapphires has increased.

The ghost town of Yogo, such as it was, has largely disappeared. The camp's modest structures were never built to last, and the decades since the mine's most recent active period have reduced them to foundations and scattered debris. What survives is the landscape itself: the narrow gulch, the dike outcrop, and the creek gravels that first revealed the sapphires to Jake Hoover in 1895.

## What Remains Today

The Yogo Gulch area retains the physical evidence of more than a century of sapphire mining, including waste rock piles, the remains of processing facilities, and the exposed dike rock that is the source of the gems. The site is on private property, and organized gem-hunting operations have been conducted there periodically, though the status of public access varies with the ownership and operating arrangements of the current mine operators.

The sapphires themselves are the most durable legacy of the Yogo camp. Yogo sapphires are sold by gem dealers and jewelers across the country, and their distinctive cornflower blue color and natural untreated quality make them identifiable to knowledgeable buyers. The Gem Gallery in Lewistown is a primary source for Yogo sapphires and maintains a collection of historical and geological information about the deposit.

## Visiting

Yogo Gulch is located approximately sixteen miles from Lewistown in the Little Belt Mountains of Fergus County. The access roads into the gulch are on private property, and visitors should not attempt to access the mine site without permission from the current operators. The Gem Gallery in Lewistown provides information about current access arrangements and is the best starting point for anyone interested in Yogo sapphires.

Lewistown offers a full range of visitor services and is the logical base for exploring the central Montana ghost town circuit. The Kings Hill National Scenic Byway, which passes through the Little Belt Mountains to the west, provides a scenic driving route through the terrain that surrounds the Yogo district.
