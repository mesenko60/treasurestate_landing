# Apex

**By Editor**

*Mineral County*

## History

The Cedar Creek drainage in Mineral County was one of the more productive silver-lead districts in western Montana, and the camps that developed along its length in the 1880s and 1890s represented a significant chapter in the mining history of the Clark Fork country. Apex was one of these camps, located in the mountains above the Clark Fork River in a district that attracted miners from across the region during the years when silver prices were high and the ore bodies in the Cedar Creek drainage were yielding consistent returns.

The camp's name reflected the mining terminology of the era. The "apex" of a vein was the point at which it reached its highest elevation and broke the surface, and the Apex Mine took its name from the geological feature that defined the property's legal standing under the mining law of the time. The apex doctrine, which held that the owner of the surface claim at the apex of a vein had the right to follow that vein wherever it went underground, was one of the most contentious legal principles in the mining industry, and the litigation it generated consumed enormous resources in the major mining districts. The Apex Mine's name was a statement of legal position as much as a geographical description.

The Cedar Creek district attracted serious development in the 1880s, when the combination of accessible ore bodies and improving transportation made the economics of silver-lead mining in Mineral County favorable. The Northern Pacific Railroad's main line along the Clark Fork River provided a connection to the smelters that processed the ore, and the camps in the Cedar Creek drainage were close enough to the railroad to make transportation costs manageable. Apex was one of several camps in the district that developed during this period, and it shared the infrastructure of roads and supply networks that the district's collective activity created.

The camp's population was concentrated in the years of peak silver prices, roughly 1887 to 1893, when the combination of high metal prices and accessible ore bodies made the Cedar Creek district one of the more active mining areas in western Montana. The men who worked the Apex Mine and the other properties in the district were a mix of experienced hard-rock miners who had worked in other Montana districts and newcomers attracted by the reports of good ore. The camp had the usual complement of boarding houses and commercial establishments that a working mining community required.

The silver price collapse of 1893 ended the district's boom as decisively as it ended silver mining booms across Montana. The Apex Mine closed or reduced operations sharply when the price of silver fell below the cost of production, and the camp contracted rapidly as miners moved to other locations. The lead content of the ore provided some economic justification for continued operation at reduced scale, and some work continued in the district through the 1890s and into the early twentieth century, but the camp never recovered the population or the activity level of its peak years.

The Cedar Creek district was revisited periodically in the twentieth century as metal prices fluctuated and processing technology improved. The Mineral County Museum and Historical Society has documented the history of the district and organized tours of the Cedar Creek area that allow visitors to see the physical evidence of the mining activity. The annual Tour of the Cedar Creek Historic Mining District, hosted by the museum, provides an opportunity to explore the district's history in the company of local historians who know the landscape and the stories it contains.

The broader context of Mineral County's mining history helps explain why the Cedar Creek district attracted the attention it did. The county's position along the Clark Fork River, with the Northern Pacific Railroad providing access to markets, made it a logical location for mining development, and the ore bodies in the mountains above the river were real and substantial. The camps that developed in the district were part of a larger pattern of mining activity that transformed the Clark Fork country in the late nineteenth century and left a legacy of abandoned mines and ghost towns that is still visible in the landscape today.

The Clark Fork River corridor that runs through Mineral County was one of the more important transportation routes in western Montana during the mining era. The Northern Pacific Railroad's main line followed the river from Missoula to the Idaho border, and the camps in the mountains above the corridor were connected to the railroad by the wagon roads that the mining companies built to move ore to the valley floor. The Cedar Creek drainage was one of several mountain districts that depended on this connection, and the economics of the district were shaped by the cost of the haul from the mine to the railroad.

The annual Tour of the Cedar Creek Historic Mining District, organized by the Mineral County Museum and Historical Society, is one of the more unusual heritage tourism programs in western Montana. The tour takes visitors through the drainage on a guided route that includes the sites of the various camps and mines that operated in the district, and the local historians who lead the tour bring a depth of knowledge about the district's history that is not available in any published source. The program is evidence of the community's commitment to preserving the memory of the mining era, and it provides visitors with an experience of the district's history that a self-guided visit could not replicate.

## What Remains Today

The Apex site is located in the Cedar Creek drainage of Mineral County, in the mountains above the Clark Fork River. The physical evidence of the mining operations includes the mine workings, waste rock piles, and structural remnants from the camp's active period. The Mineral County Museum and Historical Society has documented the district's history and organizes periodic tours of the area.

The Cedar Creek Historic Mining District is accessible via forest roads from the Clark Fork River corridor, and the surrounding landscape is managed by the Lolo National Forest. The combination of the mining history and the mountain landscape makes the Cedar Creek area a destination for visitors interested in the industrial history of western Montana.

## Visiting

Apex is located in Mineral County, accessible via forest roads from the Clark Fork River corridor. Superior, the Mineral County seat, is the nearest town with visitor services, located on Interstate 90 along the Clark Fork River. The Mineral County Museum in Superior can provide information about the history of the Cedar Creek district and the location of the Apex site.

Missoula is approximately 50 miles east of Superior on Interstate 90 and provides a full range of visitor services for those exploring the Clark Fork country.
