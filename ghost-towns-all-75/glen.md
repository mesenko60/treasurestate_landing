# Glen

**By Editor**

*Beaverhead County*

## History

The narrow valley where the Big Hole River bends east toward the Beaverhead was not the kind of country that made men rich overnight. There was no gold in the gravel bars, no silver in the hills above the river. What the valley offered was something more modest and more durable: good grass, reliable water, and a position on the natural corridor between the Big Hole and Beaverhead drainages that made it a logical stopping point for travelers moving through southwestern Montana. The settlement that eventually took the name Glen grew from that geography, not from a mineral strike, and its story is the story of a hundred small communities that the railroad built and the automobile age slowly unmade.

The first permanent settler in the area was Ozias Willis, who established a ranch and a post office in 1878. The post office was called Willis Station, a name that acknowledged both the man and the function of the place: a station on the route between Dillon to the south and the mining camps of the upper Big Hole to the north. Willis was the kind of man who appeared at the edges of the Montana frontier before the great rushes, practical and self-sufficient, building a working operation in a valley that most prospectors passed through without stopping. His post office served the scattered ranchers and travelers of the Beaverhead country in the years before the railroad arrived and changed everything.

The Utah and Northern Railway reached Dillon in 1880, bringing with it the infrastructure of the industrial age: regular mail service, commercial freight, and the possibility of shipping cattle and agricultural products to distant markets at a cost that made ranching economically viable on a large scale. The railroad's route north from Dillon followed the Beaverhead River valley, and the stop near Willis's ranch became a regular service point. The community that gathered around the station grew slowly but steadily through the 1880s and 1890s, sustained by the ranching economy of the surrounding valley rather than by any single dramatic enterprise.

The name of the community changed more than once over the decades. Willis Station became simply Willis, then Reichle, a name that reflected the German-speaking families who settled in the area during the homestead era of the early twentieth century. The final name, Glen, was adopted around 1950, a plain English word that described the narrow valley the community occupied. The Reichle Elementary School, which still serves the area's children, preserves the earlier name in the institutional memory of the community.

The community's economy was always agricultural. The Big Hole River, which runs past the east side of town, provided irrigation water for hay meadows and pasture, and the ranching operations that surrounded Glen produced cattle and sheep for the Dillon market. The river also attracted a different kind of visitor in the twentieth century: fly fishermen who discovered that the Big Hole was one of the finest trout streams in Montana, holding brown trout, rainbow trout, and the native westslope cutthroat in numbers and sizes that drew anglers from across the country. The fishing economy gradually supplemented and eventually surpassed the agricultural economy as the primary driver of visitor traffic through the valley.

Browne's Bridge, built in 1915 and listed on the National Register of Historic Places, crosses the Big Hole River near Glen and provides access to a state fishing access area. The bridge is a steel truss structure of the kind that the Montana Highway Department built across the state in the early twentieth century, and its listing on the National Register reflects both its architectural integrity and its role in the transportation history of the Beaverhead country. For most of the people who stop at Glen today, the bridge and the river below it are the primary attractions.

The population of Glen has dwindled to fewer than thirty people by the 2020 census, making it one of the smallest communities in Beaverhead County. The post office remains open, serving the ranches and the few year-round residents of the surrounding valley. The elementary school continues to operate, drawing students from a wide geographic area. But the commercial life that once sustained a railroad stop has long since departed, and the buildings that housed it have either been repurposed or removed. Glen is not a ghost town in the dramatic sense of a Bannack or a Granite, not a place where the population vanished overnight when the mine closed. It is instead the quieter kind of ghost, a community that has been slowly thinning for a century, losing people one family at a time to the cities and the changing economics of western agriculture.

The valley itself has not changed much. The Big Hole still runs clear and cold past the east side of town, the hay meadows still green up in June, and the mountains that frame the valley to the west still hold snow into July. What has changed is the human presence in the landscape, reduced from the modest but genuine community of the railroad era to a handful of families keeping the post office open and the school running.

## What Remains Today

Glen retains its post office, its elementary school, and a small number of occupied residences. Browne's Bridge, the 1915 steel truss structure on the National Register of Historic Places, is the most historically significant surviving structure in the area and provides access to the Glen State Fishing Access on the Big Hole River. The fishing access site includes a boat ramp and camping facilities managed by Montana Fish, Wildlife and Parks.

The surrounding landscape preserves the agricultural character of the valley. Brownes Lake, ten miles north on Rock Creek Road, offers additional fishing and camping in a setting that has changed little since the homestead era.

## Visiting

Glen is located on U.S. Route 91 between Melrose to the north and Dillon to the south, approximately twenty miles north of Dillon. The drive through the Big Hole and Beaverhead river valleys is one of the more scenic routes in southwestern Montana, passing through open ranch country with the Pioneer Mountains visible to the west. The Big Hole River at Glen is accessible via the state fishing access site at Browne's Bridge, and the river is fishable from late spring through early fall.

Dillon, the Beaverhead County seat, is the nearest community with a full range of services and is worth a stop for its railroad depot museum and the Western Montana College campus. The Beaverhead County Museum in Dillon provides excellent context for the agricultural and ranching history of the valley that Glen represents.
