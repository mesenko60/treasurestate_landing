# Lion City

**By Editor**

**Beaverhead County**

Lion City occupied the middle chapter of the Bryant Mining District's story, the camp that replaced Trapper City and was itself replaced by Hecla, each transition driven by the same logic of following the ore uphill. Founded around 1877 on the slopes of Lion Mountain, it was the district's most productive settlement during the late 1870s and early 1880s, a rough and lively place that the Hecla Consolidated Mining Company eventually decided was too disorderly to serve as the face of a serious corporate mining operation. When Henry Knippenberg reorganized the company in 1881 and built the planned town of Hecla a mile away, Lion City did not disappear immediately, it persisted as a satellite community, but its days as the district's center were over. The elevation at Lion City runs to roughly 9,500 feet, making it one of the highest-situated mining camps in Montana's history.

## History

The shift from Trapper City to Lion City reflected a straightforward economic calculation. The Trapper Lode, which had drawn James Bryant and his associates to the district in 1873, had been worked out to the point where the richer deposits on Lion Mountain commanded attention. The Cleve and Avon lodes, identified on the mountain's upper slopes, were yielding ore that justified the difficulty of working at high altitude. By 1877, miners had relocated from the creek-bottom camp to the new site, and Lion City had taken on the character of a full mining community, with the saloons, boarding houses, and commercial establishments that followed wherever men were earning wages underground.

The Hecla Consolidated Mining Company, organized in January 1877 by Noah Armstrong and eastern investors, acquired the key claims on Lion Mountain and began developing them aggressively. The Cleopatra Mine, the Trapper workings, and the Cleve-Avon group were the district's primary producers, and the ore they yielded was processed first at a concentrator on the mountainside and then at the smelter in Glendale, seven miles down the canyon. The tramway that Knippenberg built in 1882, four miles of narrow-gauge track connecting the upper mines to the Greenwood Concentrator, ran through terrain that had been Lion City's working ground, and the infrastructure of the company town of Hecla gradually drew the district's population away from the older camp.

Lion City's reputation for disorder was not incidental to its decline. Contemporary accounts describe it as a place where the saloon trade dominated the social life of the camp and where the company's efforts to maintain a sober, productive workforce were perpetually frustrated. Knippenberg's decision to build Hecla was explicitly framed as a move away from Lion City's environment, and the new town was designed with the amenities, a church, a school, a company store with banking facilities, that were intended to attract and retain the kind of settled workforce that a long-term mining operation required. The men who moved to Hecla did not necessarily abandon Lion City entirely, but the center of gravity shifted.

After the 1893 silver crash and the subsequent closure of the Glendale smelter in 1900, Lion City shared the fate of the rest of the district. The Penobscot Mining Company's effort to revive the upper workings between 1913 and 1914, installing a twenty-stamp mill at the Lion City site, represented the last serious attempt to extract value from the mountain. The mill ran for less than two years before the operation was abandoned, and by 1915 the district was effectively deserted. The elevation that had always made Lion City difficult to supply and to inhabit year-round ensured that no one remained when the economic reason for being there was gone.

The district's total output from 1873 to 1965 was tallied at over 656,000 tons of ore valued at more than $19.6 million, with 13.4 million ounces of silver among the metals recovered. Lion City's contribution to that figure was substantial during the late 1870s and early 1880s, when the mountain's richest ore bodies were being opened for the first time.

The physical conditions of mining at Lion City were severe even by Montana standards. At 9,500 feet, the winters arrived early and stayed late, and the supply problem that Joseph Vipond had partially solved by building a road in the lower district was never fully resolved at the upper elevation. Ore had to be hauled by wagon down to the Glendale smelter through terrain that became impassable for months at a time. The miners who worked the Cleopatra and Cleve-Avon in winter were isolated in a way that the men at lower-altitude camps were not, and the social intensity of Lion City, its saloons and its disorder, was partly a product of that isolation. When the ore was good and the wages were paid, men tolerated conditions that would have driven them away in any other circumstance. When the ore ran out, they left without looking back.

## What Remains Today

Lion City retains scattered structural remnants at its high-altitude site, collapsed log buildings, mine shaft openings, and the debris of the Penobscot Company's early twentieth-century operations. The site is more visible than Trapper City but less intact than Hecla, reflecting its position as the district's middle chapter: neither the first camp to be abandoned nor the last to hold on. The terrain around Lion City is steep and heavily forested, and the ruins are distributed across a landscape that shows the marks of both nineteenth-century underground mining and the later surface disturbance of the Penobscot era.

The broader context of the Bryant Mining District is best understood by visiting all three upper sites, Trapper City, Lion City, and Hecla, in sequence along Trapper Creek Road. The charcoal kilns on Canyon Creek, which supplied fuel for the Glendale smelter during the district's peak years, are a separate but related attraction located closer to the highway and more accessible to standard vehicles.

## Visiting

Lion City is reached via the same route as Hecla: Interstate 15 Exit 93 near Melrose, then approximately twelve miles on Trapper Creek Road into the Beaverhead-Deerlodge National Forest. The road becomes progressively rougher past the Glendale site, and four-wheel-drive vehicles are required for the upper portion. The elevation at Lion City means the site is snowbound from roughly November through April. No services or facilities exist at the site. Visitors should check road conditions with the Dillon Ranger District before departing, and should plan for a full day if they intend to visit Trapper City, Lion City, and Hecla in a single excursion.
