# Black Butte

**By Editor**

*Meagher County*

## History

Coal is not the mineral that most people associate with Montana's ghost towns. The state's mining mythology runs to gold and silver, to the prospectors who worked the placer bars and the hard-rock miners who followed the veins into the mountains. But coal was essential to the territory's industrial development, and the camps that grew up around Montana's coal seams have their own history, distinct in character from the precious-metal camps but no less significant in the story of how the territory became a state and how the state's economy was built.

Black Butte was a coal mining camp in Meagher County, situated in the country between the Castle Mountains and the Crazy Mountains in the broad valley of the Musselshell River drainage. The coal seams in this part of central Montana were not the thick, high-quality deposits of the Crow Reservation country to the southeast, but they were accessible and they were close to the railroad, and in the 1890s and early 1900s those two qualities were enough to make them worth mining.

The Montana Railroad, which pushed through the Musselshell Valley in 1896, was the catalyst for Black Butte's development. The railroad needed coal for its locomotives, and the coal seams near Black Butte were close enough to the line to make extraction economically viable. A mining operation was established, and the camp that grew up around it took the name of the prominent dark butte that dominated the local landscape, a geological feature formed by the same volcanic activity that shaped much of the terrain in this part of central Montana.

The camp was a working-class community in the most straightforward sense. The men who mined the coal were not prospectors hoping to strike it rich but wage workers employed by a company that needed a steady supply of fuel for its railroad operations. The housing was company housing, the store was a company store, and the social life of the camp was shaped by the rhythms of the mining operation rather than the speculative excitement that characterized the precious-metal camps. Black Butte had none of the saloons and gambling houses that made the gold and silver camps notorious; it was a sober, industrial place where men worked regular shifts and went home to their families at the end of the day.

The quality of the coal at Black Butte was adequate for the railroad's purposes but not exceptional. The seams were thin by the standards of the major coal fields, and the extraction costs were higher than at more productive operations. As long as the Montana Railroad needed the coal and had no better alternative, Black Butte remained viable. But the railroad's fortunes were never secure, and when the line was absorbed into the Milwaukee Road in 1908, the new owners had access to better coal sources and less reason to continue the expensive operation at Black Butte.

The camp declined through the first decade of the twentieth century and was largely abandoned by the time the Milwaukee Road completed its electrification of the mountain divisions in the 1910s. Electric locomotives did not need coal in the same quantities that steam locomotives required, and the demand for Black Butte's output fell accordingly. The families who had built their lives around the mining operation moved on to other communities, and the camp was left to the gradual processes of decay.

The homestead era brought new settlers into the Musselshell Valley in the years after Black Butte's mining operations ended, but the homesteaders were farmers rather than miners, and they had no use for the infrastructure of a coal camp. The buildings were salvaged for lumber, the mine workings were left to collapse, and the site reverted to the grass and sage that had covered it before the railroad arrived.

Black Butte's story is a reminder that Montana's ghost towns are not all products of the precious-metal rushes. The state's industrial history includes coal camps, railroad towns, and agricultural communities that flourished briefly and then declined as the economic conditions that had sustained them changed. These communities are less dramatic than the gold and silver camps, but they are no less real, and their ruins are no less worth understanding.

The Milwaukee Road's decision to electrify its mountain divisions was one of the most ambitious engineering projects in the history of American railroading. The electrification, completed in stages between 1915 and 1927, covered 656 miles of track across the Rocky Mountains and the Cascades, and it required a reliable supply of electrical power generated from the rivers of the mountain west. The electric locomotives that replaced the steam engines on the mountain grades were more powerful, more efficient, and less dependent on the coal that had fueled the steam era. For Black Butte, the electrification was the final blow: a railroad that no longer needed coal in large quantities had no reason to maintain an expensive mining operation in a remote location.

The Musselshell Valley communities that surrounded Black Butte had their own economic trajectory, shaped by the homestead era that followed the railroad's arrival. The valley's agricultural potential was limited by the same semi-arid climate that constrained farming throughout central Montana, and the homestead communities that grew up in the early twentieth century experienced the same cycle of optimism and failure that characterized the Hi-Line country to the north. By the time Black Butte's mining operations had ended, the valley was already filling with homesteaders who would themselves be gone within a generation.

## What Remains Today

The Black Butte site retains remnants of the coal mining operation, including mine waste and disturbed terrain from the extraction activities. The prominent Black Butte geological feature that gave the camp its name remains visible in the landscape and provides a useful landmark for locating the general area of the former camp.

The Musselshell Valley has a number of historical sites associated with the railroad era and the homestead period, and Black Butte fits into the broader pattern of settlement and abandonment that characterizes the valley's history. The Roundup area to the east has more substantial coal mining history and better-preserved remnants of the coal camp era.

## Visiting

Black Butte is located in Meagher County in the Musselshell Valley drainage, accessible from US Highway 12 in the general area between White Sulphur Springs and Harlowton. The site is on private land, and visitors should obtain permission before entering. White Sulphur Springs to the west and Harlowton to the east are the nearest communities with visitor services.

The Wheatland County Museum in Harlowton maintains exhibits on the history of the Milwaukee Road and the coal mining operations that supported the railroad in central Montana. The museum provides useful context for understanding Black Butte's place in the broader history of the region.
