# Cleveland

**By Editor**

*Blaine County*

## History

Cleveland, Montana, is not a mining ghost town. It belongs to a different chapter of the state's history, the homestead era that followed the mining rushes and populated the high plains and river breaks of north-central Montana with families who came believing that the land could sustain them. The story of Cleveland is the story of that belief, its origins, its brief flowering, and its gradual dissolution as the realities of the Hi-Line country asserted themselves against the optimism of the settlers.

The community developed in the early twentieth century in the breaks country of Blaine County, south of Chinook along the drainage systems that feed the Milk River. The terrain here is the kind that looks deceptively hospitable from a distance: rolling grasslands cut by coulees, with enough moisture in most years to grow grass and, it seemed to the homesteaders, perhaps enough to grow wheat. The Northern Pacific and Great Northern railroads had been pushing settlers into this country since the 1880s, and the federal homestead acts provided the legal framework that allowed families to claim 160-acre parcels and attempt to build farms on land that had previously been used only for cattle ranching.

The homesteaders who came to the Cleveland area in the first decade of the twentieth century were part of the great wave of settlement that transformed the Montana plains between 1900 and 1920. They came from the Midwest, from Scandinavia, from the eastern states, drawn by railroad promotional literature that described the Hi-Line country in terms that were optimistic to the point of dishonesty. The land was cheap, the soil was deep, and the first few years of the homestead era coincided with a period of above-average rainfall that made the country look more productive than it actually was.

Cleveland grew as the surrounding homesteads multiplied. A post office was established, giving the community its formal identity, and the services that a farming community required began to accumulate: a general store, a school, the informal social networks that sustained isolated rural communities. The community was never large by Montana standards, but it was a real place with real people who had invested their savings and their labor in the belief that they could make a life in the breaks country of Blaine County.

The drought years of the 1910s and 1920s revealed the fragility of that belief. The rainfall that had made the homestead era seem viable in its early years was not a permanent feature of the climate but a temporary deviation from the long-term average, and when the dry years returned, the wheat crops failed and the farms that had been built on the assumption of adequate moisture became unworkable. The Great Depression compounded the agricultural crisis, and the combination of drought, low prices, and exhausted credit drove families off the land throughout the Hi-Line country.

Cleveland lost its post office and its commercial functions as the surrounding population declined, and the community contracted to a handful of families who held on through the difficult years. The school closed when there were no longer enough children to justify keeping it open, and the buildings that had housed the community's commercial life were abandoned or repurposed. The landscape around Cleveland reverted to the grass and sage that had covered it before the homesteaders arrived, and the community became one of the many ghost towns that mark the high-water line of the Montana homestead era.

The breaks country south of Chinook retains a haunting quality that is different from the ghost towns of the mining districts. The mining camps were abandoned because the ore ran out or the prices collapsed, and their ruins are the ruins of industrial enterprise. The homestead ghost towns were abandoned because the land itself proved inadequate to the demands placed on it, and their ruins are the ruins of domestic life: the foundations of farmhouses, the remnants of root cellars, the occasional standing barn that has outlasted the family that built it. Cleveland is that kind of ghost town, and the country around it tells that story to anyone willing to read it.

The pattern of settlement and abandonment in the Cleveland area was not unique to Blaine County. Across the Hi-Line country, from the Sweetgrass Hills in the west to the Poplar River country in the east, the homestead era left behind a landscape of abandoned farmsteads, closed schools, and ghost towns that mark the places where the optimism of the early twentieth century collided with the realities of the northern plains climate. The communities that survived were the ones with railroad connections and enough agricultural land to sustain a viable farming economy. The ones that failed, like Cleveland, were the ones that depended on marginal land and favorable weather, and when the weather turned against them, they had nothing to fall back on.

The Bear Paw Mountains to the south of Cleveland add a different layer of history to the area. The mountains were a traditional gathering place for several tribes, and the country between the mountains and the Milk River was contested territory during the Indian Wars of the 1870s. The Bear Paw Battlefield, where Chief Joseph and the Nez Perce made their final stand in October 1877, is located in this same landscape, and the proximity of that site to the homestead ghost towns of the Cleveland area gives the region a historical depth that rewards careful attention.

## What Remains Today

Cleveland retains a small number of standing structures and the foundations of others, scattered through the breaks country south of Chinook. The landscape itself is the primary artifact of the homestead era: the disturbed soil of old fields, the remnants of fencing, and the occasional building that has survived the decades since the community's commercial life ended.

The Bear Paw Battlefield, site of the final battle of the Nez Perce War in 1877, is located in the same general area of Blaine County and is managed as part of the Nez Perce National Historical Park. Visitors to the battlefield often pass through the Cleveland area, and the combination of the battlefield and the homestead ghost towns gives the region a layered historical character that rewards careful attention.

## Visiting

Cleveland is located in Blaine County, south of Chinook in the breaks country along the drainage systems feeding the Milk River. The access roads are county roads and may be impassable in wet conditions. Chinook, the Blaine County seat, is the nearest community with visitor services and is located on US Highway 2, the Hi-Line route across northern Montana.

The Blaine County Museum in Chinook maintains exhibits on the history of the area, including the homestead era and the Bear Paw Battlefield. The museum is a useful starting point for understanding the history of the Cleveland area and the broader context of settlement and abandonment in the Hi-Line country.
