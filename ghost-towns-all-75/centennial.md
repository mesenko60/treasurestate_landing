# Centennial

**By Editor**

*Beaverhead County*

## History

The Centennial Mountains rise along the Idaho border at the southern edge of Beaverhead County, forming a wall of ridgeline that catches the first light of morning and holds snow well into June. The valley they shelter, long, flat, and drained by the Red Rock River, was known to the Shoshone and Bannock peoples as a travel corridor between the Snake River Plain and the upper Missouri drainage. When American prospectors arrived in the 1870s, they brought a different kind of attention to the landscape, and for a brief period the Centennial district produced enough silver and gold to sustain a small working camp at the foot of the mountains.

The Centennial Mining District was organized in the 1870s, during the decade that saw prospectors fan out across every drainage in southwestern Montana in search of the next Bannack or Alder Gulch. The Centennial Mountains offered a different kind of geology than the placer-rich valleys to the north. Here the ore occurred in lode deposits, silver-bearing veins cutting through the metamorphic and volcanic rock of the range. The men who worked these claims were hardrock miners rather than placer men, and they brought with them the tools and techniques of the underground: drill steel, black powder, and the patience required to follow a vein through the mountain.

The camp that grew up to serve the district was modest by the standards of the era. It sat at the edge of the valley, close enough to the mountain workings to be practical but far enough from the worst of the winter snowpack to remain accessible through most of the year. A few dozen men worked the district at its peak, supported by the usual complement of camp services: a store, a boarding house, and the informal social arrangements that characterized small mining settlements throughout the territory. The isolation of the Centennial Valley, which lay at the end of long wagon roads from Dillon and Lima, meant that supplies were expensive and news arrived slowly.

The district produced silver and gold through the late 1870s and into the 1880s, but the yields were never sufficient to attract the kind of capital investment that would have transformed the camp into a town. The ore bodies were real but limited, and as the more accessible surface workings were exhausted, the cost of following the veins deeper into the mountain began to outweigh the returns. The silver crash of 1893, which devastated mining communities across Montana, delivered the final blow to what remained of the Centennial district's working population. By the mid-1890s the camp was effectively abandoned.

The valley itself continued to attract attention of a different kind. Ranchers had recognized the quality of the Centennial Valley's grasslands as early as the 1870s, and cattle operations gradually replaced the mining economy as the primary human activity in the area. The Red Rock Lakes, at the western end of the valley, became known to naturalists in the early twentieth century as one of the last nesting grounds of the trumpeter swan, a species that had been hunted nearly to extinction across its former range. In 1935, a survey confirmed that a small population of trumpeter swans still nested at the lakes, and President Roosevelt established the Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge to protect them. The refuge, expanded over subsequent decades, now encompasses much of the valley floor.

The establishment of the refuge effectively closed the Centennial Valley to the kind of development that had transformed other parts of southwestern Montana. The old mining claims in the mountains above the valley were never reworked on any significant scale, and the camp that had briefly housed the district's working population disappeared without leaving much trace in the historical record. What the valley preserved instead was something rarer than silver ore: a functioning ecosystem largely intact from the period before European settlement, home to moose, pronghorn, sandhill cranes, and the trumpeter swans whose survival had given the refuge its reason for being.

The Centennial district's story is in some ways the inverse of the great Montana mining narratives. It did not produce a Granite or a Bannack, did not generate the fortunes that built Helena's mansions or Butte's copper empire. What it produced was modest and quickly exhausted, leaving behind a landscape that nature reclaimed so thoroughly that the mining chapter reads today as a brief interruption in a much longer story.

## What Remains Today

The mining camp itself has left no visible surface structures. The mountain workings above the valley are marked only by the disturbed ground of old prospect pits and the occasional collapsed adit, largely hidden by the dense vegetation of the Beaverhead-Deerlodge National Forest. The valley floor is dominated by the Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge, which manages the area primarily for wildlife habitat and limits public access to designated areas and roads.

The landscape that the miners would have known, the flat valley floor, the willow-lined river channels, the abrupt rise of the Centennial Mountains to the south, is essentially unchanged. The trumpeter swans that nest at the Red Rock Lakes are the most dramatic living presence in the valley, and their survival is a direct consequence of the refuge's establishment.

## Visiting

The Centennial Valley is reached via Montana Highway 87 from Lima, or via a longer unpaved route from West Yellowstone. The road through the valley is unpaved and can be impassable in wet conditions; a high-clearance vehicle is strongly recommended. The Red Rock Lakes National Wildlife Refuge maintains a visitor contact station at the refuge headquarters near Lakeview, and staff can provide current road and wildlife conditions.

The best time to visit is late May through September, when the roads are passable and the wildlife is most active. The valley is one of the most remote and least-visited corners of Montana, and its isolation is a significant part of its appeal. Visitors should come prepared for self-sufficiency; the nearest services are in Lima, roughly forty miles to the west.
