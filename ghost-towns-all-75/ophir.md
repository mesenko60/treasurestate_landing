# Ophir

**By Editor**

*Powell County*

## History

The creek that gave Ophir its name runs cold and clear out of the Garnet Range foothills, dropping through a narrow canyon before joining the Blackfoot River drainage in what is now Powell County. Prospectors working their way east from the Deer Lodge Valley in the early 1860s found color in its gravels almost by accident, and the name they chose, drawn from the biblical land that supplied Solomon's temple with gold, said everything about their ambitions. Whether the creek would live up to that name was another question entirely.

The first serious placer work on Ophir Creek began around 1860, making it among the earliest mining ventures in what would become Montana Territory. The men who arrived that first season were experienced enough to recognize good color when they saw it, but the source of the gold, the lode deposit from which the placer material had eroded, remained stubbornly hidden. They worked the bars and benches through the early years of the territory, sluicing what they could find, but the yields were modest compared to the spectacular strikes at Alder Gulch and Confederate Gulch that drew the great crowds of 1863 and 1864. Ophir Creek remained a quieter place, worked by men content with steady rather than spectacular returns.

The camp that grew up along the creek shared its name with the drainage. It was never a large settlement: no newspaper, no courthouse, no grand hotel. What it had were the essentials: a few cabins, a store that doubled as a post, and the kind of informal social order that characterized small placer camps throughout the territory. The men who worked Ophir Creek in those years were largely anonymous in the historical record, their names appearing only in the occasional assay record or land filing preserved in the Deer Lodge County archives.

The search for the lode source of the placer gold continued for nearly two decades. Prospectors sank test shafts on the hillsides above the creek, followed quartz stringers into the rock, and came away with promising samples that never quite justified the cost of development. The geology of the area, a complex mix of metamorphic basement rock cut by younger intrusive bodies, made the ore bodies difficult to trace. What looked like a promising vein at the surface often pinched out within a few feet of depth.

It was not until 1879 that serious lode mining finally took hold in the Ophir district. Prospectors working the west side of Ophir Creek found several quartz veins carrying gold and silver values sufficient to warrant development. The ore was a mix of quartz with pyrite and chalcopyrite, and the better samples assayed at values ranging from forty to one hundred dollars per ton, respectable figures for the period, though not spectacular by the standards of the great silver camps to the south. A small mill was constructed to process the ore, and for a time the district attracted enough attention to sustain a modest working population.

The most significant development came in 1900, when the Victory Mine was discovered on the west side of Ophir Creek, roughly two miles above the old placer camp. The mine was developed beginning in 1906 by operators who believed they had finally found the principal lode source that the earlier prospectors had sought. The gold-silver ore proved to be of good quality, and some of it was shipped directly to smelters while the remainder was worked at a small mill constructed at the site. The waste dump and mill tailings were later reworked using the cyanide process, a technique that had become standard in the industry by the early twentieth century. All told, the Victory Mine produced approximately forty thousand dollars in ore by 1912, when excessive groundwater flooding the lower workings brought operations to a halt. The operators lacked the capital to install adequate pumping equipment, and the mine was abandoned.

The end of the Victory Mine effectively ended Ophir as a functioning settlement. The few remaining residents drifted away, the cabins were left to the weather, and the creek returned to the quiet it had known before the first prospector arrived with his pan. The Ophir Mining District was formally recorded by the Montana Bureau of Mines and Geology as having produced gold and silver from its first active year of 1860, making it one of the longer-lived small districts in the state, not because of any single dramatic strike, but because the ground kept yielding modest returns to patient men across five decades.

## What Remains Today

Almost nothing of the original camp survives at the surface. The Victory Mine site retains its waste dump and the disturbed ground of the mill tailings area, but the structures that once stood there have long since collapsed or been removed. The creek itself is the most enduring presence, running through a landscape of lodgepole pine and Douglas fir that has largely reclaimed the ground the miners disturbed. Ophir Creek Road provides access into the drainage, but the mine sites require cross-country travel on foot and a tolerance for dense forest and steep terrain.

The Ophir Mining District is recorded in the USGS Mineral Resources Data System, which lists the elevation of the principal workings at approximately 5,400 feet. The district falls within the Beaverhead-Deerlodge National Forest, and visitors should be aware that the area is managed primarily for timber and watershed values rather than recreation.

## Visiting

Ophir is reached from Deer Lodge via roads heading east into the Garnet Range foothills. The terrain is rough and the roads are not maintained for passenger vehicles beyond the lower drainage. A high-clearance vehicle is advisable, and the area is most accessible in summer and early fall. There are no interpretive signs, no maintained trails, and no facilities of any kind. This is a destination for those who find meaning in the landscape itself, in the sound of a creek that prospectors named for a biblical land of gold, and in the quiet that has settled over the hills since the last miner walked away.

The town of Deer Lodge, the Powell County seat, is the nearest community with services. The Old Montana Prison complex in Deer Lodge offers excellent historical context for the broader settlement patterns of the region.
